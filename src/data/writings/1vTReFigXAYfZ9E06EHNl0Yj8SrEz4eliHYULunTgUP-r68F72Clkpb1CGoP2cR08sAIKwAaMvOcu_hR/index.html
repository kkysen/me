Ms. Weller

European Literature

12/16/2015

The True, Turned, Turbanned[1] Turk: Iago

In Shakespeare’s Othello, Iago meticulously devises a complex plan to undermine Othello by insinuating an affair between Cassio and Desdemona.  Although he appears to exact vengeance upon Othello for promoting the young, inexperienced Cassio to lieutenant over himself, this is merely his cover to hide his true, hidden agendum of wreaking havoc upon the Venetian military, such that his lord, Sultan Mehmed II, the Kaiser-i-Rum[2], may emerge as the ultimate victor with Venice subjugated at his feet.  Having traveled to France to learn the brilliant espionage tactics of the Universal Spider[3] under the tutelage of King Louis XI himself as part of the secret French-Ottoman alliance[4], he is now infiltrating the ranks of the Venetian army to strike at the much vaunted general Othello, who opposes Ottoman dreams of a vast Turkish lake[5] and is masterminding the European opposition to the Ottoman navy.  Wishing to gain naval hegemony in the Mediterranean, and having just conquered Constantinople and Illyria[6], they aim to sink Venice and ultimately strike Vienna from the southwestern flank.  Iago has been tasked to eliminate the immediate threat, Othello, without leaving a trace of a Turkish plot.  Therefore, Iago employs his spider tactics[7] to ensare Othello in a meticulously spun web of lies and deceit in order to poison his mind.

Just after the Turkish fleet stages its destruction in the storm, fooling all the Venetians on Cyprus, Iago begins to plan out the warped new character he will create for Desdemona.  Having no one to practice on but Desdemona herself, he rehearses how he will denigrate Desdemona in front of Othello, saying, “Come on, come on! You are pictures out of door, bells in your parlors, wildcats in your kitchens, saints in your injuries, devils being offended, players in your huswifery, and huswives in your beds” (2.1.122-125). Then accidentally letting his true nature slip out, he says “Nay, it is true, or else I am a Turk” (2.1.127) in affirmation of his earlier slander.  Since the previous derogatory exclamations merely slander and are thus false, it follows that Iago must be a Turk.  

In fact, Brabantio has also been included in this plot.  Desperate to win his daughter back from the Moor, he is willing to go to any length to succeed, including betraying his city and allying with a Turk.  Working with Iago, he gets the last word in with Othello, planting a seed of doubt about Desdemona’s fidelity: “Look to her, Moor, if thou hast eyes to see. / She has deceived her father, and may thee” (1.3.133-134).  But although winning Desdemona back is his top priority, it is still not in his nature to betray Venice, and thus he also tries to warn the Doge[8] by dropping this hint: “So let the Turk of Cyprus us beguile” (1.3.241).

After he sets in motion the first stage of his plan, getting Cassio demoted, Iago outlines the remainder of his strategy:

Divinity of hell! / When devils will the blackest sins put on, / They do suggest at first with heavenly shows, / As I do now. For whiles this honest fool / Plies Desdemona to repair his fortune, / And she for him pleads strongly to the Moor, / I’ll pour this pestilence into his ear: / That she repeals him for her body’s lust; / And by how much she strives to do him good, / She shall undo her credit with the Moor. / So will I turn her virtue into pitch, / And out of her own goodness make the net / That shall enmesh them all. (2.3.370-382)

In saying this is Satanic thinking, Iago is acknowledging that much of his plan relies on French spider tactics, for the French, and all Christians as well, were seen as evil incarnate in the eyes of the Turks[9].  Yet Iago still recognizes the genius of the Universal Spider, and thus he realizes he must assume such Satanic plotting to succeed in his goal, thereby temporarily embodying the nature of a devil as he commits a sin using French, and thus devilish, spider tactics.  As he goes on explaining the intricacies of his plan, he mentions how he will “pour this pestilence into his ear” (2.3.376) and “out of her own goodness make the net / That shall enmesh them all” (2.3.381-382), which are the modi operandi of the Universal Spider’s vast network of spies, evidence of Iago’s training under Louis XI.  Therefore, in order to engineer the credulous suicide of Othello, Iago is forced to take on the devilish form of his mentor to draw out the virtue of Othello and the Venetians and turn it black, spinning a duplicitous web of chicanery to ensare all his enemies.

But as events come to denouement, the web begins to unravel.  With the truth about Iago’s nefarious plan against Othello revealed, Othello is finally able to connect the dots and deduce Iago’s true nature of a Turk at the close of his suicide speech: “a malignant and a turbanned Turk / Beat a Venetian and traduced the state, / I took by th’ throat the circumcisèd dog, / And smote him, thus” (5.2.414-417).  Othello has finally discovered the identity of his arachnoid parasite, the malignant Turk named Iago who has wreaked such havoc upon Othello and is “More fell than anguish, hunger, or the sea” (5.2.425) while hidden under his turban of false honesty and trust.  Iago has thus beaten the Venetians and disgraced the Republic, but it is not he who is the circumcisèd dog (Iago would instead be called an execrable spia[10] or something similarly abominable); rather, it is Othello who is the circumcisèd dog.  Being a “circumcisèd dog” is a weak insult[11] that Othello would use against himself, thinking himself guilty of murdering his wife, just before he commits suicide and joins Desdemona.

Thus in the wake of these deaths, Iago’s triplicity comes to light: for one, he is honest Iago, the Moorship’s trusted ancient, for omega[12], he is a vengeful soldier out for the blood of his general, and for omega squared[13], he is a deeply-hidden Turkish spy with French ties working to consume Venice from within.  With his true agendum exposed, Lodovico condemns Iago to a life of torture: “For this slave, / If there be any cunning cruelty / That can torment him much and hold him long, / It shall be his” (5.2.391-394).  It is through these years of torture that the Venetians are able to extract all of the Turkish plans for Europe, thus enabling Venice to move against the sleeping giant that was about to awaken and unleash its terror upon the underbelly of Christendom.  

Consequently, as Mehmed II and his successors attempt to bring their master plan to fruition, they are extremely successful along the Barbary Coast[14], where the Moors are lacking Othello, their ingenious general loaned out to the more active Venetian army.  However, their operations in the European theater fare vastly different.  Armed with inside knowledge of the Turkish master plan, the Venetians are able to warn the Austrians about an impending invasion and siege of Vienna[15] in 1529 by Suleiman the Magnificent[16], thus stopping the rampaging Ottoman army from entering the doors to Europe.  Then 42 years later, they amass an allied Venetian-Spanish fleet, which delivers a crushing blow to the Ottoman navy in the Battle of Lepanto[17], forever ending dreams of a Turkish lake.  Having seen the unparalleled rewards of having a vast espionage service, the Venetians build up one of their own, which proves crucial during the War for Crete[18], during which Venetian intelligence is able to repeatedly undermine Turkish operations.  It is these Venetian spies who are able to discover a second Ottoman invasion of Europe and warn Jan Sobieski’s Winged Husaria[19] to save Vienna[20] in the nick of time from being razed to the ground.

Iago keeps his deadly subterfuge hidden throughout most of the play, ingeniously utilizing his spider tactics to push Othello to assassinate himself, only to overlook Emilia and allow her to reveal the truth that unravels the intricate web of lies.  Consequently, Iago’s Turkish allegiance and the Turkish master plan are discovered, allowing the Venetians to gain the upper hand against the Turks in the wars to come.  At first, only Iago’s duplicity is revealed, but eventually Othello uncovers his deeper triplicity, for Iago is not a cunning madman bent on revenge, but he is a calculating spy defending his empire. Such a topic that so closely integrates the Turkish menace to Christendom would have been enthralling to Shakespeare’s English audience.  Although living in England and far away from the Turks, their distance had only served to inflate the rumors of the extreme savagery of the Turkish infidels; no one had forgotten the Siege of Vienna, and Lepanto[21], “the nearest run thing you ever saw in your life”[22] on the sea, was fresh in their minds, having occurred only 30 years prior to the publication of Othello.  But including such a realistic threat was not the main aspect that captivated Victorian England, it was the timeless political intrigue manifested as manipulating espionage.  Shakespeare has been much applauded for examining themes that are immortal and inherent to human nature, ideas that will resonate with today’s audience just as much as they did with Victorian England.  In this play, he chose the eternal conflict of espionage and political intrigue, hostilities that have plagued humanity for millennia and will continue to endure as long as humans live on the face of the earth.

[1] This is the Shakespearian spelling of “turbaned” appearing in 5.2.414.

[2] Mehmed II (1432-1481) was an Ottoman sultan who conquered the virtually unsiegeable Constantinople, thus ending the Byzantine Empire, and much of the Balkans, and consequently was known as Mehmed the Conqueror and Kaiser-i-Rum, the latter of which means the Caesar of the Roman Emperor.  In the Balkans, he conquered Serbia, Morea, Moldavia, and Albania, although he was repeatedly thwarted by Venetian Crusade-like counteroffensives.

[3] Louis XI (1423-1483) was a Valois King of France who was known as the Universal Spider, because his enemies often accused him of spinning webs of lies, plots, and conspiracies, as well as having an extensive espionage network.  Later in life, he made designs on the northern Italian city-states, ultimately leading to Charles VIII invasion of Italy and the subsequent French involvement in Italy for centuries to come.

[4] In real life, Francis I of France and Suleiman the Magnificent created an alliance to oppose the otherwise unchecked power of Charles I of Spain and V of the Holy Roman Empire, an Austrian Habsburg who had married into the Trastamara family in Spain to inherit both empires.  In this essay, I’ve proposed that this alliance was created earlier between Louis XI and Mehmed II to oppose the growing power and alliance between the Habsburgs and Spanish Trastamaras, such that France would be poised to take Milan and the other northern Italian city-states, and the Ottomans would be ready to defeat the Venetian fleet, conquer Venice, and strike at Vienna from the southwestern flank.

[5] People often referred to a Mediterranean under complete Ottoman control as a Turkish lake.

[6] Illyria is a region of the western Balkans from classical antiquity.  It is the setting of Shakespeare’s Twelfth Night and thus I have used the name Illyria instead of the more contemporary names.

[7] “Spider tactics” is the name the author of this paper has given to the hypothetically/assumably arachnoid espionage tactics employed by Louis XI’s extensive spy network.

[8] The Doge of Venice was the chief magistrate and leader of Venice, probably equivalent to the Shakespearian use of the “Duke” of Venice in Othello.

[9] The fact that the Turks saw Christian Europeans as evil incarnate is mostly false, but it was wholly believed to be by Victorian England and much of Christendom, reflecting their opposing view of the Turks as savage infidels.

[10] A derogatory Italian word for spy.

[11] The Berbers, from whom the Moors are descended, had practiced circumcision centuries before Mohammed and the rise of Islam, and thus predate and Muslim or Turkic circumcision.  Therefore, Othello wouldn’t see circumcision in such a bad light, and consequently seen it as only a relatively weak insult, not something despicable.

[12] Instead of a bidirectional phrase like “on one hand,...on the other hand…,” a tridirectional phrase is used instead.  The bidirectional phrase suggests the Two opposing Roots of Unity that are spaced 180° apart, 1 and -1.  Likewise, the tridirectionality of triplicity suggests the Three Roots of Unity, 1, ⍵ (-½ +i√3 /2), and ⍵2 (-½ +i√3 /2) that are spaced 120° apart on the complex plane.

[13] See 10.

[14] After the reign of Mehmed II, the Ottomans rapidly conquered North Africa, mainly the Barbary Coast where the Moors live, during the reigns of Selim I and Suleiman the Magnificent.

[15] In the wake of the Battle of Mohacs in which Suleiman the Magnificent soundly defeated the Hungarian army in 1526 and annexed Hungary, the Ottoman army continued into Europe to siege Vienna, culminating in the failed Siege of Vienna in 1529.

[16] Suleiman I (1494-1566), commonly known as Suleiman the Magnificent in the West, was the longest-reigning Ottoman sultan who ruled at the apex of the size and power of the Ottoman Empire.  He lead armies to conquer much of the Middle East, North Africa, and Hungary, only being stopped at Vienna (see 13), in addition to gaining naval hegemony over the Mediterranean, Red Sea, and Persian Gulf.

[17] The Battle of Lepanto (1571) was a naval battle fought between the Ottoman Empire and the Holy League, a Christian-European coalition organized by Pope Pius V that included Spain, Venice, and other maritime Italian forces.  The Holy League was created to protect the Venetian colony of Famagusta in Cyprus from the besieging Turks.  When the Ottoman commander broke the armistice, the allied fleet sailed to attack, and the Ottoman fleet sailed from their naval station in Lepanto, to meet in the Gulf of Corinth.  It ended in a decisive European victory, shattering the aura of invincibility the Turks had at the time and killing many of their veteran sailors.  Although the Ottomans quickly rebuilt their fleet, they could never replace the experienced sailors and the Ottoman navy never regained its former glory.

[18] The War for Crete (1645-1669) was the longest war fought between the Republic of Venice and the Ottoman Empire.  It was fought over Crete.  In this protracted war, Venetian espionage gave them the upper hand, with a combination of superior Venetian intelligence, and the capability to incite rebellion among the Ottoman Christian subjects and assassinate and sabotage the Turks proving vital to the combined war effort.

[19] The Polish Winged Husaria (or Hussars) were the elite heavy cavalry force of Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth.  Although not large in size, they were the one of the best equipped and trained heavy cavalry forces ever, routinely routing forces over ten times their size.  Armed with kopia, 6.2 m long lances (longer than most pikes), they were even capable of utterly destroying infantry pike formations, unassailable by most other cavalry forces.  During the Battle of Vienna, they were commanded by Jan Sobieski and routed the massive Ottoman army.

[20] The Battle of Vienna (1683) was the second siege of Vienna by the Ottoman Empire.  Grand Vizier Kara Mustafa Pasha led an Ottoman army 90,000-300,000 strong to besiege Vienna, which was garrisoned by only 11,000 Austrian soldiers.  After months of besieging, including fighting in tunnels dug beneath the walls, an allied force of German and Polish troops arrived, including the Winged Husaria, under the command of Jan Sobieski that was able to soundly defeat the remaining Ottoman army.

[21] In Othello, Shakespeare’s allusions to the Battle of Lepanto are numerous: his estimate of the size of the Turkish fleet (200 galleys) and the movements of the Turkish fleet, especially around Rhodes, are both identical to those described by contemporary historians (Knolles), and Lepanto was also fought over Cyprus.  Not a strict historian, Shakespeare took the liberty of distorting the historical timeline to also include Lepanto as a later event where the Venetian knowledge of the Turkish master plan proved vital to their victory.

[22] Duke of Wellington quote on the Battle of Waterloo
