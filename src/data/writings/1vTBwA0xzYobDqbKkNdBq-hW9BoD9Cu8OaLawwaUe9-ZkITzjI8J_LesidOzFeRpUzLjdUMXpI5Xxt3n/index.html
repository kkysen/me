Assimilating Into the American Interface: A New Immigration Paradigm

In The Namesake, Jhumpa Lahiri presents the life story of a second-generation Indian American, Gogol.  Following his life, Lahiri carefully examines Gogol’s ongoing identity crisis, torn between the Indian culture his parents retain at home and the new American culture he is exposed to every day.  Although many differences seem to focus in on his name, Gogol is constantly trying to navigate between two disparate cultures and his name is often a scapegoat, the starkest reminder of how he is split between his family, heritage, and the rest of his country.  Throughout the “string of accidents” that repeatedly exacerbate his identity crisis, Lahiri’s main focus is on describing Gogol’s reaction, his thoughts, and the consequences, but she never tries to put forth a solution.  By the end of the book, we are not even sure if Gogol has completely overcome his identity crisis and has come to peace with himself; all we know is that Gogol has rediscovered his namesake and his father’s legacy.  But in no way does Lahiri try to hint at a solution, at how Gogol should have dealt with his crisis differently, or how his parents should have raised him differently.  By offering no real solution to Gogol’s identity crisis, rather than simply omitting an important piece of the story, Lahiri has ingeniously crafted a novel that succeeds in lyrically exposing first-generation Asian Americans to the many challenges their children face.  By not imposing on them specifically how they should change, Lahiri is able to reach a much broader, more receptive audience more willing to reconsider how they raise their children to better include the perspective of their children.

The identity crisis faced by Gogol is in no way unique to Indian Americans; it is the fundamental struggle faced by any immigrant to any foreign place.  When one is exposed to two dominant and imposing cultures, at some point, there must be a reconciliation that resolves one’s identity, or else one will continue through life torn between two identities and cultures.  In Gogol’s case, this crisis is particularly crippling, lasting nearly 32 years.  There might be a remedy to his personal case, yet Lahiri chooses to omit it because there will never be any one solution that fits all.  Consequently, this story is able to avoid alienating the vast majority because it doesn’t conclude with a narrow-minded solution applicable to only a select few, one that could possibly make some feel criminalized by sending the message, “Why didn’t you do this?  It made your child suffer.”  Instead, Lahiri is able to maintain the universality and resonance that makes her novel so influential and resounding in the immigrant world.

The message Lahiri sends to all immigrants, both first- and second-generation, is to expand their horizons and consider the challenges everyone faces.  Children should try to better understand their parents’ point of view, how they sacrificed everything to come to America, and their lifelong struggle to adapt to and find acceptance in an alien world.  Instead of rejecting their parents’ way of life, children could learn to better appreciate all the obstacles their parents have overcome for them.  At the same time, parents could be more aware that their sacrifices won’t necessarily benefit their children.  To the child, the biggest sacrifice their parents can make is to accept American culture and refrain from imposing their culture wholesale on their children.  After uprooting their entire lives and moving to an alien country, parents may feel like they have sacrificed enough, that they are entitled to at least keep their culture and raise their children to appreciate their heritage and be grateful for what their parents sacrificed.  They feel a great nostalgia for their former lives and don’t want to give it up, but it is this act of not completing the immigration 100% that so often troubles their children, because then they are left strung between two conflicting cultures.  By relaxing this pressure, they can help lessen the identity crisis and avoid burdening their children with the need to achieve and fulfill their parents’ expectations and dreams.  That’s not to say parents need to raise their children in a house completely devoid of another culture, but it should be presented in a way that complements rather than antagonizes the unavoidable American culture surrounding them.  The easiest way to accomplish that is for the parents to begin to let go of their former lives and assimilate themselves, because if they can’t start resolving their own identity crisis, then how can their children be expected to?

My family has been a prime example of this.  This is why I find it so hard to relate to Gogol or any of the immigrants in The Namesake, because I was raised in an environment free from suffocation by imposing and clashing cultures.  My dad follows much of the same story: his father (nicknamed Oh-Oh by me) was born in Calcutta, moved to Boston to attend MIT for grad school, and had my dad in the summer of 1968, and then my dad went on to go to architecture school at Columbia, but that’s where the similarities end.  Oh-Oh was first and foremost a scientist and he brought only little more than the international values of science with him to America.  He may not have been the best father, but he made one redeeming decision: he kept the burden of his Indian past and culture off my dad’s shoulders, allowing my dad to develop his own views and values.  Oh-Oh didn’t alienate his Indian side—he still visited his home every year—but he made the wise decision to allow my dad to develop his own American identity without any potentially debilitating clashes.

In my life, this is true to an even greater extent.  My mom, born and raised in Taiwan, moved to Long Island with her family when she was thirteen.  Although much younger than Ashoke and Ashima, she continued living with her completely Chinese parents as opposed to the Gangulis who started fresh.  I don’t know exactly what happened by the time she gave birth to me, but I know that as my mom, she has managed to be a lot more American than Chinese in raising me.  Her and my dad’s way of raising me aren’t that different, especially compared to many other Chinese or other immigrant parents I see, and even to the Gangulis.  She’s never put too much pressure on me to ever do anything, allowing me enough freedom and space to develop my own identity without being bombarded by conflicting views, values, and cultures.  That’s not to say her Chinese side is hidden from me; it has always been there, free for me to explore, just never imposed on me.  

An integral part of achieving this is the existence of an interracial marriage.  Not only does this mean I’m already mostly assimilated having a native-born father and that it is harder to impose as strong a culture if they are divided between husband and wife as well, but that my mom already possessed the willingness and drive to let go of much of her past and start a new family, one that would not be dominated by any one outside culture, but one that emerges completely American.  Ultimately, what she, along with my dad, have succeeded the best in, something I hope new immigrant parents can learn from, is extracting the core values from their cultures and instilling this set of values in me.  Instead of trying to impose a huge, bulking culture on me with all of its rules, beliefs, and idiosyncrasies, she has given me the values upon which I can build my own identity and culture that is distinctly me, but that at the same time flawlessly integrates into my American life.

If I do say so myself, my success and lack of any perceived identity crisis at this stage in my life speak for themselves of the merits of such an approach.  Who knows what my future holds, but right now, I attribute much of my success to and am extremely grateful for this.  Gogol had to struggle with his parents’ Indian culture imposed on him at home coming into conflict with the American culture surrounding him every day, and this forced him to spend many years after leaving his parents trying to find his own way and drive.  But because I have been free to develop my own culture founded on a set of shared values, I have from the beginning been able to tailor it to me.  Unique to me, it can function flawlessly with my every thought, allowing me to find my own drive to motivate myself in the absence of any pressure.  By not imposing upon me any hard and unyielding pressures, they have actually driven me further.  I put much more pressure on myself than they ever could, and this is a drive that will hopefully last a lifetime.  While my identity and culture are certainly not complete, they have a strong foundation that will allow me to make my way through life in the absence of parental support without having to spend a decade desperately trying to reformulate my core identity.

It is this notion of passing on only a core set of values, not an entire, suffocating culture, that allows a child to develop their own unique but compatible identity that I believe to be fundamental to a successful assimilation, especially in America.  America was founded not on a complex culture or shared history, but upon a set of values linking disparate states and backgrounds.  It is not the complete culture that makes you an American, but the underlying core values, upon which can lay almost anything.  Thus, in order to assimilate into America, one must target the underlying values.  The bulky culture on top can remain, but for a child, it is hard to reconcile such cultures founded upon different values with American values.  Instead, it would be easier for the child to build his own culture on top of American values and then later choose how to integrate with other cultures.

I’m a big computer science lover, so I couldn’t help but put this in terms of Object Oriented Design, in which everything is an object that contains methods that can interact with other objects.  The “blueprints” for these objects are classes, which can be extended to another class, inheriting all the methods of the parent class with the option to add more.  But the problem with this is that it only supports single inheritance well.  In other words, if a class tried to extend multiple parent classes, then there might be a clash between the implementation of methods.  The solution is interfaces, which define how an object can interact with other objects, but not how the methods are actually implemented.  Thus a class can implement multiple interfaces and resolve the method clashes, allowing disparate classes to implement the same interface.  We can then define an American interface that contains a set of values (methods), but it is up to each individual to define these methods themselves, to decide how they will interpret and live up to these values.  Therefore, while people might have different cultures (classes), they will still all be American.  We Americans can then unite as one collection, America, simultaneously a melting pot of cultures and homogenous in our core values.  But when immigrant parents try to impose their culture on their children, their children are left torn between conflicting aspects (methods) of the cultures in a terrible attempt to achieve multiple inheritance of classes.  Perhaps if more immigrants assimilated into this American interface, passing on only a core set of values and not an entire, imposing culture onto their children, then immigrant life in America would be rendered much smoother, helping to ease xenophobic tensions and dissolve generational rifts by showing that we are all Americans because of the values we hold dearest.

Revision Reflection

In writing my original think piece, I had trouble findings any ways in which I could relate to Gogol or other parts of the story.  Even though I’m three-quarters Asian and my mom is an immigrant, I’ve never really encountered any of the distinctly immigrant or Asian American experiences seen in The Namesake.  I’ve been raised very American and in a very inclusive and tolerant city, and thus I’ve never been in any meaningful, relatable experiences.  Even my name, which is probably more rare and odd than even Gogol, has never been a point of contention, has never been laughed at by others, or has never come under much negative scrutiny by myself.  Therefore, I chose to focus more on my thoughts about the book instead, which I had plenty of.  Since it is a “think” piece, I thought if I just wrote about my thoughts and they were interesting, that would be good.  I guess it didn’t come across that way as much, reading more as an essay than as my personal thoughts, which they still are.  This was your major criticism, that I didn’t talk nearly enough about myself in the piece, and adding this formed the majority of my revision.

I realized that the idea I developed at the formed end of the piece was pretty much exactly what my mom has done for me, the reason why I have so few of the experiences Gogol has so many of.  Therefore, I tried mainly to extend upon what I had before, revising that only minimally, and instead focusing on telling how my and my family’s story fulfills this idea and then going from there.  This makes my think piece quite long, but I couldn’t not include my own personal experiences and thoughts at the end, nor could I chop off the beginning, because my whole addition builds off my former concluding point.  Having developed this idea of passing on a core set of values upon which the child can build rather than imposing on them a fully-formed, massive culture, I tried to explain its extreme relevance to what it means to be an American, ending what I see as an amazing metaphor to a programming paradigm.  Hopefully I explained it well enough, because I feel it eloquently captures the crux of what it means to be an American and how American can still function so well as a unified melting pot.
