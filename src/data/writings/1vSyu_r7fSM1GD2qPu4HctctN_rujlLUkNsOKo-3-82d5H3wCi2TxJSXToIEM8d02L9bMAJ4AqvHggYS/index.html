Eighteen Shoes For a Russian Foot Fetish

I like to think that at some of those stores, they bought the shoes of Ivanka Trump, a fashion designer born in A.D. 1981.  She was the daughter of Donald Trump, the POTUS famous for his huge golden towers.  When she was thirty-five years old, she was captured by a Russian operative during a raid by Putin’s secret agents.  Days after days of cold, bleak skies passed as she stared out the window at the streams of gray, lifeless factory workers trudging to work.  She was forced to join these workers, laboring mindlessly all day.  She tried to stay behind, but there was no avoiding it.  Soon she became pregnant and gave birth, but after twelve weeks when she started to head back to work, her communist boss asked her, “What are you doing back so early?  Your son’s only 12 weeks old.  You still have 8 weeks left.”

“I thought I only get 12 weeks of unpaid leave.  20 is so great!”

“Wait, you thought that was unpaid?  I’m so sorry, I forgot to give you your salary.  You get 20 weeks of 100% paid maternity l. eave, 18 months of 40% pay, and up to 3 years of unpaid leave[1].  Where the hell did you come from where you only get 12 weeks of unpaid leave?”  

Ivanka left the factory skipping in joy.  In her father’s company, she had only received 12 weeks of unpaid leave, she was being given 3 years!  His other employees fared no better—if they got paid at all—but at least she didn’t get “grab[ed]... by the pussy,” although he has admitted to wanting to have sex with her.  Compared with her father’s country, perhaps those Russian communists and Putin stoically riding on horseback weren’t so bad after all.

But still, day after day, she saw factory workers trudging to work in identical ugly brown shoes different only in how much they were falling apart.  Those were the only shoes she saw for days on end to the point that she became convinced they were the only shoes that existed in Russia.  They all wore the same heavy, drab clothing to shield against the cold, they all ate the same plain, dull food, and drank so much vodka that even alcohol became dreary.  Even their facial expressions were too stoic—let alone Putin’s—to the point that longed for the neverending supply of bizarre facial expressions of her father’s face.  Then one day Putin brought her to a party.  She finally saw real Russian shoes, but they were barely any better; they might not have been falling apart, but they were still as ugly as her dad’s hair.  The shoes made Ivanka reel in disgust and their ugliness made her ache for the beautiful and elegant shoes she had made in the US.  It disturbed her so that she could not concentrate on her own thoughts.

When she had her second child and got another three years off, she set her mind to making her own shoes.  Night after night the shoes filled the streets no matter how far away she walked.  She hid in her room working on her own shoes but could not sleep through the nightmares of the same ugly shoes over and over again.  One day Putin and his friends discovered her hidden shoes.  They marveled upon these beautiful shoes.  They couldn’t understand what their use was—they seemed to be capitalistic—but they could understand their beauty and elegance.  Her children watched silently, not understanding their mom’s capitalistic drive.  But when she left the room, they became fascinated by the shoes, trying them on all day.

After twelve years among the Russian communists, Ivanka Trump was ransomed for a billion dollars along with the Trump Tower and married to Jared Kushner so that her father would have real estate investor descendants.  She brought her new lines of shoes back from the frozen lands, and one of the three that is now being sold is “Eighteen Shoes For a Russian Foot Fetish.”

Author’s Note

After reading the Cai Yen story at the end, I found it intriguing that Kingston chose to relate Americans to Xiongnu barbarians and immigration to kidnapping.  Raising this new parallel, she is retelling much of the same Chinese American immigrant story about her mother in a new, simpler context.  But I saw one major missing part to this new story, another “attack” on Chinese misogyny, as Frank Chin would call it, as well as a possible origin story to the Woman Warrior.  Nomadic societies, in general, have been known to be considerably more egalitarian.  In the case of Steppe nomads like the Xiongnu and closely-related Scythians, this was true to an even greater extent.  Chinese sources have described Xiongnu women routinely fighting alongside men, and the Greeks turned the astounding numbers of female fighters among the Scythians into the fabled Amazons, the epitome of a Woman Warrior.  To miss this great opportunity here was perhaps a big mistake, something I considered filling in.

However, not too much is known about these ancient societies, and I wanted to have a more modern, applicable take on this void.  If Frank Chin’s accusations had any truth, and Kingston did denigrate Confucian Chinese society while promoting white Christian America, then I wanted to come at this from a new angle, one that flips the tables and slanders America as the misogynistic culture and praises our greatest “barbarian Xiongnu,” communist Russia.  In light of the recent election, this seems to hold even truer.  Although the ideal of gender equality under Communism was never reached, it was still much better than the US.  Just look at abortion: while we’re still fighting over it and heading in the wrong direction, the Soviet Union legalized it almost a century ago.  In this parody of the story of Cai Yen, I tried to expose this contradiction of Kingston’s laud of America as a better society and the widely perpetuated label of Russia and communism as our despicable, irredeemable enemy.
