To Achieve Diversity in NYC’s Specialized High Schools,

Bring Back Gifted and Talented Programs

With NYC mired in a racially charged debate about the equitability of the Specialized High School Admissions Test (SHSAT), a multiple choice test still appears to be the most unbiased form of admissions.  There are no biased graders or admissions officers that can be surreptitiously influenced.  Everyone remains on a level playing field, so whoever is the smartest and studies the most should score the highest.  So why are the SHSAT results so racially stratified?  This year, only 7 black students out of 895 were admitted to my alma mater, Stuyvesant High School.  Only 1% of Stuyvesant is black and 3% Hispanic, compared to 74% Asian-American and 12% white, even though 70% of the public school system is black or Hispanic.  It seems clear there must be some racial bias or form of racism at play.  The city, led by Mayor de Blasio has already been trying to diversify the specialized high schools by increasing the Diversity Program, which offers admission to economically disadvantaged students who score just below the cutoff.  But none of it is working, and the racial stratification is only increasing every year.

So last June, Mayor de Blasio proposed a new plan to scrap the SHSAT and admit the top 7% from each middle school, which would make Stuyvesant 45% black and Hispanic.  As much as this plan is welcomed by many, there has been intense backlash in Asian-American and alumni communities, who fear they are being punished, that the SHSAT is already as meritocratic as possible, and that removing the test will lower Stuyvesant’s standards and deny entrance to countless Asian-Americans.

However, it wasn’t always this way.  During the 1970s and 80s, the diversity problem had nearly been solved.  In 1976, the percentage of black and Hispanic students at Stuyvesant reached an all-time high of 12% and maintained that level for the next decade and a half.  By 1989, Brooklyn Tech had reached an astounding 51% black and Hispanic student body.  But then something changed, and these peak levels began plummeting, reaching the all-time lows of 4% at Stuyvesant today.

Since the peaks of racial diversity beginning in the 1970s, the one constant has been the SHSAT, so it couldn’t have been the test itself that caused the precipitous decline in racial diversity.  Instead, in the 1990s, then-Mayor Giuliani ended the then ubiquitous gifted and talented (G&T) programs in middle schools.  Soon afterward, the percentage of black and Hispanic students at Stuyvesant and the other specialized high schools began to plummet.  In an effort to eliminate such ability tracking because they were deemed discriminatorily stratifying, the city unwittingly spelled the doom of racial diversity at its most elite public institutions.

The primary argument against tracking, and the main reason why it was eliminated in the first place, is that tracking perpetuates privilege.  Gifted students are given preferential treatment, and privileged students are more likely to be admitted to G&T programs, and that only gives them a further advantage.  In fact, international studies like PISA found that tracking doesn’t improve overall education and only further stratifies.  However, NYC is a very different case.  Schools are geographically zoned, so already racially segregated neighborhoods generally have racially and socioeconomically homogenous schools.  The fear of tracking, of privilege perpetuating inequality, is thus rendered null in most schools here, because in a racially and socioeconomically homogeneous school, there is little diversity of privilege to begin with.  But by identifying the few gifted students, putting them in the same classroom, and teaching them with the same rigor and aspiration as the most troubled students, they might be able to overcome the odds to gain admission to a school like Stuyvesant.

While tracking may have given the “gifted” preferential treatment, the absence of tracking also hurts these gifted students, because while the struggling students receive extra help, the gifted students need no such help and are thus taught and challenged less.  Instead of creating true equality, eliminating tracking only creates equity, i.e. equal outcomes.  It is this notion of equity, not equality, that PISA measures and is too often focused on when true equality of opportunity should be the goal.

The NYC DOE is not treating students at each school the same, but teaching them to the same level with the goal of achieving equal educational outcomes.  They are stamping out all specialization, but then expecting students to suddenly specialize in high school.  Instead of diversifying each middle school by having both honors and remedial tracks, the reality is we have middle schools that are drastically different from each other, but each of which is very homogeneous within.  Our city is left with good middle schools that are essentially like specialized high schools, except students cannot get in through a meritocratic test, but are selected based on what neighborhood they live in.  The lack of tracking is perpetuating geographic stratification, punishing the exceptional students at poorly performing schools and preventing them from having the resources to study for and ace the SHSAT.

It should be obvious that we cannot expect that each student will have the same educational outcome due to the natural variety of academic aptitude.  But by focusing on equity, we only focus on educating the underperforming students to help them get closer to the average while leaving the top students without challenges, causing them to regress to the mean.  By doing so, we realize the goal of equity: to make everyone migrate to the average so that we all become the same, equal.  But the more we champion equity (i.e. equality of outcome) the more we lose equality of opportunity, which is certainly not desirable.  Instead of equity in education, we need diversity of educational outcomes.  We need to help the underperforming students, but not by abandoning the top students.  By fostering true equality of opportunity, which means teaching everyone, whether it be the struggling student or the G&T student, we can more successfully help every child to rise their greatest potential.  So even though tracking may not be the most equitable solution, we are aiming to achieve natural diversity through equality.

To successfully diversify our schools, we must re-implement middle-school tracking in NYC, taking care to ensure it is flexible, narrowly-tailored to specific subjects, and not introduced too early.  In doing so, we will begin solving the extreme racial stratification in the specialized high schools in a constructive, non-racially divisive way that avoids pitting minority against minority while still preserving academic rigor and meritocracy.  We will be simultaneously offering a lifeline to the thousands of other exceptional students that we routinely ignore that don’t have the privilege of attending a school like Stuyvesant.  Our mayor needs to think hard about resolving the lack of diversity in our specialized high schools with real funding and a strategy that has a proven record.

Khyber Sen graduated from Stuyvesant High School in 2018 and is now finishing his freshman year at Columbia University.
