The Fourth Person

“Oh, my God!” I muttered, “Oh, my God!  It was Gatsby, that damned bootlegger!  I knew those rumors of him killing a man once were true.  Now’s he’s gone and done it again, killed my wife, that cousin to the devil.”  I sped along the road toward West Egg, to that mansion owned by Gatsby, or more likely by the prodigal son of von Hindenburg.  I had just left Tom’s house.  There’s definitely something off about that man, something he’s not telling me, but he told me enough.  He told me where to find that yellow car, he told me where to find the man who killed my wife.  

I drove faster than that yellow car, flooring the gas.  “Why won’t this damn car go any faster.  I’ll get that bastard.  I knew he was a killer.  When I get to know a thing I know it.  It was Gatsby in that car.  She ran out to speak to him and he wouldn’t stop.  Now he’s going to get what’s coming to him.”  I knew I was going to kill that bastard, no doubt about it.  But I didn’t know how I should kill him yet.  I thought about running him over if I was so lucky to find him outside.  The perfect revenge: an eye for an eye, a life for a life, death by that cursed yellow car for death by my car.  God would be watching it all, those giant, wan blue eyes would be looking down upon me, but they would applaud my revenge.

But I managed to see through my rage for a second and realize he wouldn’t just play into my hands and walk in front of my car.  I had my gun and I could use it.  A single shot would do the job.  Boom!  Dead as Myrtle.  All that was left was finding Gatsby.  Then I would have my revenge.

I parked my car in the woods surrounding Gatsby’s house and snuck in through the side.  I couldn’t find him anywhere, so I looked in the garage for that damned yellow car.  I took me a bit but I found it soon enough.  Just sitting there, that open car looking all smug.  He didn’t even try to hide it!  He probably would’ve had the nerve to bring it to me for repair, that arrogant prick.  I picked up a hammer on a shelf and started smashing that fender that killed my wife, that windshield behind which Gatsby sat staring down Myrtle, and that steering wheel that he didn’t even try to turn.  I screamed and screamed,  “Gatsby!  You killer!  You killed my wife, you bastard!  Show yourself!  You don’t even have the nerve to face me, you murderer!” for what felt like hours, but he was nowhere in the mansion of his.  So big that no one, not even his servants, heard me screaming.  I went back outside and was creeping back to the trees when I heard a little splash.  “The pool, the pool!  He’s in the fucking pool!” I muttered under my breath as I ran back toward the pool.  That’s where I saw him, came face to face with the man who killed my wife.  What was he doing, you ask?  He wasn’t even running away.  He was fucking laying there on an air mattress sunbathing after he just killed my wife.  The coward!

*        *        *

Laying there on my pneumatic mattress, chugging a bottle of liquor, my first drink since meeting Dan Cody, I slowly drifted off staring up at the sun.  The sun morphed into that familiar single green light at the end of Daisy’s dock, the same one that I watched every night for the last five years.  But this time as I stared into the light, Daisy actually emerged with Tom in tow.  Behind them appeared a ship on the horizon.  I couldn’t make out the letters on its side at first, but as it pulled into the dock, I made out Tuolomee painted along its side and saw that ever familiar face of Dan Cody at the helm.  But it wasn’t James Gatz who got on board this time and transformed into Jay Gatsby, it was Tom and Daisy, hand in hand, who got on board, Tom clumsily stumbling his way on deck while Daisy floated up to the helm as if by magic.  Suddenly I wasn’t lying in my pool anymore.  I was still on my mattress, stranded in Long Island Sound as the Tuolomee embarked.  I saw Tom and Daisy standing at the helm, steering the ship directly on course for me.  The shadow of the hull soon engulfed me as Tom and Daisy bore down on me.  “Jay Gatsby” would soon be dead, broken up like glass against Tom’s hard malice and Daisy’s sophisticated, thrilling scorn.

The sunlight streamed into my eyes as I rolled onto my side, wakened by a rustle in the woods.  I looked across the pool, seeing what a grotesque thing a rose is and how raw the sunlight was upon the scarcely created grass.  A new world, material without being real, where poor ghosts… Then I saw it, the poor ghost of a man, breathing dreams like air, drifting fortuitously about, an ashen figure gliding toward me through the amorphous trees.  I couldn’t see anything more than that ashen figure, but I could just tell, it was the husband of the woman Daisy had killed.

Then came the screams of a fellow lover scorned: “Gatsby!  You killer!  You killed my wife, you bastard!  I’m going to kill you!  She never did nothing to you, but when she ran out to speak to you, you didn’t even slow down, you fucking murderer!  Stand up and face me like a man, or I’ll hunt you down on that stupid raft of yours and kill you anyways!”

I could understand where he was coming from; he was not an old sport like Nick, he was a provincial man, but a fellow lover scorned nonetheless, and he would go to any length to avenge her death.  There would be no chance of expostulation; he had been pushed past the breaking point and revenge to the death was his only path.

Laying there on my mattress I was eerily calm.  I knew I could easily escape.  I was floating in the middle of the pool, a good fifty yards away from Wilson.  He couldn’t get a clear shot with me laying down so far away, and with his truculent rage, he wouldn’t be able to keep his hands steady.  I could just roll off into the pool, and by the time Wilson could get a clear shot, my staff would’ve heard the shots and Wolfsheim’s guards would come running.

I had stared death in the face before.  In the Argonne Forest I had taken two machine-gun detachments so far forward that there was a half mile gap on either side of us where the infantry couldn’t advance. We stayed there two days and two nights, a hundred and thirty men with sixteen Lewis guns, and when the infantry came up at last they found the insignia of three German divisions among the piles of dead.  I had emerged a great war hero, awarded medals of valor from even little Montenegro.  But this time was different.  In the Great War, Daisy was still waiting for me back home in Louisville, and her love was as strong as ever.  I fought for her and escaped unscathed.  But now if I managed to escape, what future would I have.  Daisy had betrayed me, left me for that hulking Tom.  And the police would soon be after me as well, and betraying Daisy was out of the question.  She may have stopped loving me at some point, but my love for her had never waned.  What hope did I have?  Tom and Daisy would sail off into the abyss, I would be thrown in jail, and I would never be able to see Daisy again.  There was nothing left for me in this world without Daisy.  So I stood up straight and proud, staring into a face of rage as bullets ripped through my chest, but a face that unknowingly understood exactly what I was going through.

*        *        *

Running out toward the pool, I started yelling all sorts of obscene curses at Gatsby.  He was in the middle of the pool laying down, so I couldn’t get a good shot.  I told him to stand up and face me like a man so that I could shot him right through the heart.  And he did.  And I shot him straight through the chest seven times.

Time seemed to slow down then as I stared into that eternal, forlorn face that told me at once that he understood everything I was going through.  Him standing up was completely unexpected.  The man who ran over my wife without even slowing down would never let himself be killed by that, he would’ve rolled into the pool and tried to escape.  I was certain he had killed my wife, but now a seed of doubt was creeping into the back of my mind.  I had just killed an unarmed man in cold blood.  A man who had stood up in surrender, and I just shot him in the chest seven times without even a second thought.

I dropped my gun on the hard concrete and ran forward to the edge of the pool.  Maybe there was some way all my shots had missed his heart, I thought.  But no, all I saw was a laden mattress moving irregularly down the pool, leaving in its path a wake of blood pouring out from torn flesh of a once great man, ignited by the liquor he was drinking that I must’ve shot.  I kneeled down on the pool’s edge and began to pray for forgiveness.

*        *        *

After the first shot went straight through my heart, I felt much lighter, as light and delicate as Daisy.  I was floating up, out of my body and up toward heaven.  I looked down upon my body and saw myself ignited in flames.  The holocaust was complete in the name of Daisy, the second Buchanan to cause a holocaust.

Just as I was about to fade into the light, another figure caught the corner of my eye.  The woman I had just sacrificed myself for because I thought she had given up on me, she had just returned for me.  I watched, almost in slow motion, as she scampered over to grab Wilson’s gun and hold it up behind his head.

“You dumb fool, I’m the one who killed your wife!  You just killed the love of my life!  I’m going to kill you, how dare you sleep with her and just run her over to get rid of her!”

Bam!  A single shot tore through my supine body, shattering my dream and ending my life just as I heard a second shot.
