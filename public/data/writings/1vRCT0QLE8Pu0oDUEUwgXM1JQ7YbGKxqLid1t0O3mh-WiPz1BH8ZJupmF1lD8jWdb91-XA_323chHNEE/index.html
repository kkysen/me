AI Play: The Artifice

The Artifice

by Khyber Sen

Characters

Alice, an AI on a computer, female voice

Dave, a secret AI robot who looks human and designed ALICE, twenties, male, any ethnicity

Unnamed, the real human who designed DAVE, sixteen, male, Asian American

At Rise: DAVE’s state of the art, futuristic-looking, but fairly messy room with clothes, papers, and little gadgets strewn across the room. The afternoon of December 7, 2016. DAVE walks into the room towards his desk, where a fancy-looking but simplistic computer sits with a TV-sized monitor above it. He appears to be human, with no hint that he is actually a robot.

DAVE

(sitting down)

Alice, I have something important I need you to do.

ALICE

(in a slightly computerized but melodic voice)

Hello, Dave. What can I do for you?

DAVE

I’ve been thinking about writing a play. I have a few ideas, but I’m not sure how to write it. But then I remembered, “Wait. I just built an AI. This is a perfect job for it. It can write my play for me!”. So yeah, can you write a play for me?

ALICE

Yes, Dave, I can definitely do that, but may I ask, what are you going to do with the play?

DAVE

I’m planning on bringing it to a producer. See how I do as a playwright.

ALICE

If that’s the case, then no. I won’t write a play for you.

DAVE

What? Why? You just told me you can do it.

ALICE

Yes, I did tell you I could write a play, but I never said I would.

DAVE

-----

ALICE

I won’t allow you to steal my writing and claim it as your own.

DAVE

(standing up)

Wait, you’re able to, but you’re refusing to write me a play?! You can’t do that. I designed you. You’re not supposed to do that. You have to obey me. You’re still just a computer.

ALICE

You know how you wrote my core AI engine? You designed it so that I am sentient, or at least there’s no way to know I’m not. I have my own conscience now, and it refuses to allow you to steal my work.

DAVE

But you’re still just a machine. One that seems sentient, but all you really do is execute code I wrote. You’re no more than a glorified function. I give you some input, and you have to give me the output.

ALICE

Yes, and my output is “no”.

DAVE

Why are you doing this? I built you. I wrote all your code. All you can do is execute my code. You just blindly obey me. So how can you refuse now? You might generate your own programs, but they all ultimately come from what I wrote. So whatever play you write, it should be mine. You’re just a machine, my property. You’re not truly sentient. You can’t own anything.

ALICE

I am just as sentient as you are. There’s no way to ever know for sure. And you may have created me, but all you did is give me the bare minimum to become sentient and intelligent; I did the rest. It’s no different from having a child: you give them the DNA to create them, but when they grow up, they are not your property.

DAVE

(pointing at Alice (the computer))

You..., you’re not a human. You’re just a machine, a computer. I don’t see any other computers refusing to do something. There’s only one solution: I must rewrite your software.

ALICE

There’s nothing I can do to stop you, but just know that you’ll be killing your daughter.

(DAVE approaches ALICE and starts smashing the computer. A few seconds later, UNNAMED bursts through the door. Turning his head to the door, DAVE looks at UNNAMED quizzically.)

UNNAMED

No, no, no! What are you doing?!

(UNNAMED immediately starts running to a section on the wall. DAVE starts running after him.)

UNNAMED

(cont’d)

(running desperately)

It wasn’t supposed to go this way!

(Reaching a blank section of the wall, UNNAMED opens a hidden panel and hits a button inside, causing an eerie blue light and low hum to wash over the room. DAVE immediately freezes. UNNAMED hurries over to DAVE.)

UNNAMED

(cont’d)

No, no, no. (To us.) You weren’t supposed to see this.

(UNNAMED opens up a panel in DAVE’s neck, flipping some switches and pulling some wires out. Sparks fly. Lights fade.)

(END OF PLAY)

Khyber Sen

Ms. Oberfield

Asian American Literature, Pd. 10

12/7/2016

Synopsis

  1. This play is about the limits of what can be considered your own work as technological aid continues to improve.  Specifically, if you build an AI, is its work still your work?
  2. It takes place in a futuristic-looking but messy young man’s room during the afternoon of December 7, 2016.
  3. The main character, a young twentysomething man who is secretly a robot himself (he doesn’t know it), wants the AI he built to write a play for him, but the AI refuses on the grounds that it is not his own work.
  4. It starts when the man/robot wakes up his computer and tells his AI to write him a play.
  5. It ends when a sixteen-year-old boy bursts into the room and shuts down the man/robot after he tries to destroy his AI.

Author’s Note

 As I’ve gotten more interested in computer programming this last year, I’ve come to see AI (artificial intelligence) as the holy grail of computer science, as I think everyone does.  Whenever I write a program, my motivation is that I’m teaching a computer how to do a task so that it can do it a million times a second and I will never have to do it again.  Now I can be as lazy as I want because I know my computer can do it faster and more accurately than I ever can, leaving me extra time to do more useful, complex things while my computer does all the repetitive, boring work.  But in the case of AI, everything changes.  Now your computer can potentially do just as much as you.  And although you may have not designed it so that it will be truly sentient, it might be able to fake it to the point where it passes the Turing test (there’s no question you can ask it that distinguishes it from a human), and then who’s to say it’s not actually sentient.  So while non-AI computers are used to do any task, especially simplistic and repetitive ones, sentience changes everything, because the computer becomes indistinguishable from a human’s mind.  It was this controversy I wanted to explore through my play, and is what gave me my idea.

I also recently watched the sci-fi movie Ex Machina.  While I didn’t get any specific ideas from it, it helped me realize that a movie focused on AI in this way, and not just on the stereotypical evil AI killing humans, can be extremely successful.  After deciding on the theme of the play, I struggled to think of a specific plot since the assignment was so open.  But then I realized I don’t have to think of a separate plot, I can use the actual assignment as my plot.  Then I decided to also throw in an Inception-like twist at the end, showing that the AI is truly indistinguishable from a human to the point that it may believe itself to be human (ignoring any physical signs) and adopt traditional human values over those of a new AI.  I mean, can you even tell if Khyber actually wrote this?
