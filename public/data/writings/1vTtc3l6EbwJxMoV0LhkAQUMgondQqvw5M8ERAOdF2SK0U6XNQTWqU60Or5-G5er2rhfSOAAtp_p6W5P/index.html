A Journey Through Time: 10 Tiny Decisions That Changed the Course of History

A Journey Through Time

10 Tiny Decisions That Changed the Course of History

From No Roman or Mongol Empire to Axis Victory or WWIII

Learn Why This Didn’t Happen

In this fascinating trip through time, you will journey to ten times the fate of the world was held in someone’s strategic act or decision.  From strategic blunders that cost someone the war and the existence of an empire to decisions that could have triggered WWIII, join us on this once in a lifetime journey to learn all the hidden turning points that completely changed the course of history.

A Fragile History

Held Up By the Decisions of a Select Few

Have you ever wondered about the butterfly effect, about how a single flap of a butterfly’s wings can drastically change the future.  Well, that might not ever happen, but there are certainly many turning points throughout history whose causes have been a single strategic decision.  There are of course many accidental ones, but what’s so interesting about an accident?  What you want to go back in time to see is all the ones that have been decided solely by a human mind.  In this trip through time, you will be able to experience firsthand the ten most important strategic decisions, the fascinating preludes that influenced such crucial watershed moments, and the either horrific or peaceful immediate consequence.

You will get the chance to explore the events that enabled small cities to rise from the dust into sprawling empires, the multiple times the fate of humanity hung in balance because of the imminent threat of nuclear war, and the many decisions that led to the death or miraculous survival of a nation in its infancy.  On the way, you will get to witness some of the most amazing, ingenious,and infamous battles to have been ever fought from one where the victors built pyramids of skulls out of their enemies to leave a stark reminder to its future enemies to a cavalry charge so epic that Tolkien modeled his ultimate Battle of the Pelennor Fields after it and still couldn’t even begin to compare to the real one.  You can experience all of this up close and in person if you decide to join us on our action-packed, death-defying journey through time immemorial.

The most important, really the only, reason to learn history is to understand how the present world came to be, and learn all the mistakes and successes of the past so that you can avoid the mistakes and repeat the successes.  By coming on this trip, you will receive an express tour on exactly that: you will learn the key events that, had they gone even a tiny bit differently, would have led an unrecognizable world today, and by witnessing the many strategic blunders as well as meeting the many unspoken heroes in the past, you will learn exactly what and what not to repeat today so that you can live a better, more productive life.  And although it is against the law and we must arrest you if you try to do so, it might be possible for you to escape and influence one of these watershed moments and drastically change the world as we know it.  We are offering you the opportunity to witness for yourself the moments that have defined the present, what can trump that?

Day One

The Battle of Midway, 10:16, June 4, 1941

Fly your own Dauntless dive bomber along with Wade McClusky’s bomber squadron during the Battle of Midway, watching the climactic battle between the US Pacific Fleet and the Imperial Japanese Navy for dominance of the Pacific.  

At 04:30, witness the prowess of the Japanese naval aviation as Admiral Nagumo orders the launch of 108 aircraft in only 7 minutes for an attack on Midway.  Watch the American counterattack at 07:10, and see a B-26, after being heavily damaged by antiaircraft fire, dive straight at Nagumo on the Akagi carrier, only to narrowly miss him.  Understand the thinking that went into the decision to abandon Japanese carrier doctrine, and instead of keeping half of the air wing in reserve for an anti-ship attack, equip them with land-based bombs for a second attack to knock out the remaining American forces on Midway.  But then the Japanese spotted the three American carriers, and decided to re-equip his bombers with carrier-attack bombs.  Despite this, everything still seemed to be going right for Nagumo: the initial American counterattack from Midway had failed, it had taken over an hour for the US carriers to launch 117 aircraft, many squadrons had gotten lost and were forced to return to their carriers, and the torpedo bombers were slaughtered by the Japanese Zeros.  The US’s last hope lay in their final bomber squadron, Wade McClusky’s Dauntless dive bombers.  But they, too, had launched in the wrong direction and were reaching their range limit past which they wouldn’t have enough fuel to return.  Fly alongside this heroic group as they decided to continue searching, knowing that if they didn’t find the Japanese fleet soon and drop their bombs, they would be stranded at sea.  Then at 10:16, witness McClusky spot a Japanese cruiser returning to its carrier group, and tail it back to the Akagi, Kaga, Soryu, and Hiryu, where you will see at 10:25 the heroic bombers go into a steep nosedive and successfully bomb and sink the Akagi, Kaga, and Soryu.  Witness how the heroic decision of a squadron commander completely reversed the course of the war, going from what seemed to be an inevitable Japanese victory to the complete and utter defeat of the Japanese carrier group, forever destroying the ability of the IJN to wage an offensive war in a matter of 6 minutes.  According to military historian John Keegan, it turned what was about to become a humiliating defeat into “the most stunning and decisive blow in the history of naval warfare.”  If not for the courage of Wade McClusky and his squadron, three US carriers likely would have perished in the Japanese counterattack and Midway would have been left nearly defenseless, allowing Japan to realize its dream of the Greater East Asia Co-Prosperity Sphere, too large and too entrenched for any American counterattack.

Day Two

The Battle of Cannae, August 2, 216 B.C.

Journey back to the days when the future of Rome hung in the balance, when Hannibal Barca of Carthage had amazingly crossed the Alps with his army including elephants, the epitome of “Carthage as a spear pointed at the heart of Rome” when Hannibal won an amazing victory of annihilation against the largest Roman army ever assembled.

In the aftermath of Hannibal’s previous two stunning defeats of Roman legions at Trebia and Lake Trasimene, watch the military genius that was Hannibal plan the annihilation of eight Roman legions, the largest Roman army to ever be assembled at that time.  Understand how he invented the pincer movement, a tactic that would lauded by the greatest modern generals for its result as the archetypal battle of annihilation, and used as model by Napoleon, Schlieffen, and blitzkrieg.  Outnumbered and war-weary, the Carthaginian mercenary army was able to pull of an astounding victory.  By using a controlled, feigned retreat to pull the mass of Roman infantry in towards the center, sending his strongest African infantry to encircle the Romans on the flanks, and having his superior cavalry ride through the Roman cavalry in order to circle back and encircle the Romans from the rear, Hannibal was able to completely destroy the Roman army, killing 75,000 out of 85,000 legionnaires and capturing the remaining 10,000.  Considered one of the greatest battlefield maneuvers by military historians, Hannibal’s genius in the Battle of Cannae put Rome in a dire place and near capitulation, with its entire army utterly destroyed as an effective fighting force.  But then you will witness Hannibal’s greatest mistake that cost him his dream of seeing Rome at its knees when he was on the verge of achieving it.  That night, witness the confrontation between Hannibal and Maharbal, his highly vaunted cavalry commander.  See Maharbal express his wish to march on Rome at dawn, and Hannibal say, “I commend your zeal, but I need time to weigh the plan which you propose,” to which Maharbal replied, “No one man has been blessed with all God’s gift.  You, Hannibal, know how to gain a victory; you do not know how to use it.”  Never again would Hannibal get the chance to threaten Rome, for with the city itself strengthen and Rome implementing the Fabian Strategy to refuse to fight Hannibal in a pitched battle, Hannibal and his dissolving army wandered around Italy until Rome was able to raise a new army and march on Carthage itself, where Scipio Africanus would perfect Hannibal’s own pincer movement and use it to annihilate Hannibal’s army in Zama and defeat Carthage.  Here you will see how an unprecedented victory and amazing genius were squandered by not capitalizing on the fleeting advantage.  Following the battle, you will also be able to interview Hannibal himself, to understand how he planned such a massive victory and what decisions went into denying Maharbal wish to march on Rome.

Day Three

The Battle of Kiev, September 19, 1941

Witness the Wehrmacht’s greatest victory of WWII, which became the battle that spelled their ultimate defeat.

On August 7, the Wehrmacht began the ultimate realization of Hannibal’s tactic of the pincer movement, surrounding Kiev in the largest encirclement in the history of warfare.  On September 19, you will witness the Germans capture 665,000 Soviet soldiers in the destruction of 43 Red Army divisions, concluding their greatest victory during the war.  But before the encirclement, German generals had pleaded to Hitler to allow them to skip Kiev and continue onto Moscow before the brutal Russian winter set in.  There was only enough time for one major operation, so they had to choose between Kiev, a likely victory, and Moscow, a gamble that if victorious might have capitulated Stalin’s government.  Hitler chose Kiev, because he wanted to deprive Russia of her industrial areas rather than seek the decisive knockout blow in Moscow.  Then return to August 20 to watch the vaunted German generals Franz Halder, Fedor von Bock, and Heinz Guderian discuss their impending ultimate defeat out of their greatest victory, berating Hitler for his interference in the military planning in which he knew nothing.  If the talented German generals had ran Operation Barbarossa and Hitler remained only the political leader, then the full might of the Wehrmacht would have been brought on Moscow, and if it capitulated before winter, then the Soviet Union might have lost the war, and history would never be the same.  To fight an entrenched Nazi Germany on a single front without a single bridgehead is a daunting task, and the Cold War might have been between Nazi Germany and the United States instead.  It wasn’t the decision to invade Russia that immediately spelled the defeat of Nazi Germany; they still could have easily pulled it off, as long as their generals commanded the army, not Hitler, and skipped the big breadbasket to strike at the top, for it is not enough to “kick in the door”, they “have to [cut off the head] and [then] the whole rotten structure will come crashing down.”

Day Four

The Battle of Trafalgar, October 21, 1805

Watch how Napoleon, the genius that he is, completely dismiss Robert Fulton’s offer to build a fleet of steamships, submarines, and torpedoes that can make the sailing ships in the Royal Navy obsolete, and then pay the price at the Battle of Trafalgar, when Lord Horatio Nelson thwarted all of Napoleon’s plans for an invasion of Britain.

When you go back in time to the aftermath of the French Revolution, to the ascendancy of Napoleon Bonaparte, and the time of all his amazing victories of annihilation against the allied armies of Europe, you might want to see the Battle of Austerlitz, because “the Battle of Austerlitz is the grandest of all I have fought.”  But that is just more of the same battles of annihilation.  You should really go see Napoleon’s conversation with Robert Fulton that exposes a new side of Napoleon.  He is not the romantic hero whose military genius knows no bounds, but just a normal prejudiced man whose dismissiveness towards new technology cost him his grand empire.  It is not his ill-fated invasion of Russia that spelled the downfall of the First French Empire, it is this decision: “You would make a ship sail against the winds and currents by lighting a bonfire under her decks?  I have no time for such nonsense.”  You will be able to witness this costliest mistake of Napoleon’s, as well as discuss with Robert Fulton and Napoleon their thoughts on his decision.  Then you will be whisked away to see the consequence of Napoleon’s strategic blunder as it comes back to haunt him: the annihilation of the larger allied French and Spanish navy by the Royal Navy with Lord Horatio Nelson at the helm, ready to capitalize on Napoleon’s mistake yet again after already defeating him in the Battle of the Nile.  See how Nelson goes against all conventional naval doctrine and split his fleet into two columns that will smash into the Spanish-French fleet awaiting destruction.  Taking a page out Napoleon’s book, Nelson decided to arrange his fleet into columns instead of a line as Napoleon had done with his infantry.  Instead of assembling into a line in order to maximize their ability to fire cannons, he put them in columns, thus allowing greater mobility and the ability to charge and break the French lines, where the English were able to open fire on the exposed and split flanks of the French fleet.  After the battle, you will return to Paris to meet Napoleon himself, and see how such a genius reacts to losing his chance of conquering Europe only because he dismissed an engineer’s radical idea.  Without Napoleon’s careless dismissal of futuristic weapons technology, British naval hegemony would not be certain, and we might all be speaking French today.

Day Five

The Mongol Invasion of Khwarezmia, 1219-1222

Watch how an arrogant Shah beheaded Genghis Khan’s ambassadors, turning a ruler with peaceful intentions turn into a Genghis Khan hell bent on revenge against the Khwarezmid Empire, and incurring the wrath and merciless Mongol horde.  But then see how Sultan Iltutmish of Delhi wisely learns from the Shah’s mistake and denies asylum to the last ruler of Khwarezmia, leaving him and his remaining army to be slaughtered by a vengeful Mongol army.

You will witness the consequences that a scorned gift basket had on the course of history.  Genghis Khan, after uniting the warring Mongol tribes, had immediately turned to conquering his neighbors for security, conquering the Xi Xia and brutally sieging Zhongdu (now Beijing), and to send a message to potential enemies: do not incur the wrath of the Mongol horde.  But after his initial annexations, Genghis Khan sent the neighboring Shah of Khwarezmia a message seeking peaceful trade: “I am master of the lands of the rising sun while you rule those of the setting sun.  Let us conclude a firm treaty of friendship and peace.”  Then you will be able to see firsthand the arrogance and stupidity of Ala ad-Din Muhammad for believing Genghis Khan couldn’t do anything to him.  After a local governor arrested a Mongol caravan for alleged conspiracy, Genghis Khan sent an envoy to the Shah of two Mongols and one Muslim.  The Shah then executed the caravan members, killed the Muslim ambassador, shaved the Mongol ambassadors’ heads, and sent them back to Genghis Khan with the head of the Muslim ambassador.  Here you will be able to meet the legendary and infamous Genghis Khan and truly understand his actions and rationale.  Don’t be scared.  He only builds pyramids of skulls out of his enemies heads, and only then when it is teach his future enemies not to resist.  If you are a friend or a worthy enemy, then he is your most valuable ally.  Then you will learn the best taught lesson in history: do not kill Genghis Khan’s ambassadors; if you do, then you are signing the death sentence of your entire nation.  With the full might of the Mongol horde, with all its talented generals, deadly cavalry, and new siege weapons from China, Genghis Khan personally led his armies into Khwarezmia, splitting up into many armies, one of which was given the sole objective to find and kill the Shah.  Then you will watch in quick succession the descent of Genghis Khan, “the flail of God, sent to punish them for their sins,” upon the great cities of Bukhara, Samarkand, Urgench, and Gurjang.  At Bukhara, the Mongols razed the city to the ground and then beheaded anyone taller than a fencepost.  At Samarkand, the citizens were ordered outside, where they were executed and pyramids built out of their skulls.  At Urgench, each of 50,000 Mongol soldiers were tasked with executing 24 Urgenchians.  And at Gurjang, the Mongols flooded the city and then executed the survivors.  Within two years, the once mighty Khwarezmid Empire ceased to exist, and only the son Jalal al-Din Mingburnu and the remnants of the Khwarezmid army were left fleeing to the Delhi Sultanate.  Here you can watch how Sultan Iltutmish of Delhi learned his lesson: with Mingburnu and his army on the run to the Indus, he denied him asylum in India.  Had Iltutmish accepted his fellow Muslim ruler with open arms against the foreign Mongol invaders, Genghis’ wrath unleashed upon Khwarezmia would have followed him into India.  Although many have said that India would not fall under a strong ruler, it has also been said to never invade Russia in the winter, never invade Afghanistan, and never fight a land war in Asia, the Mongols did all three with little resistance.  Without the prudence of Iltutmish, India would not have been spared the same Mongol wrath that ended the Golden Age of Islam with the decimation of centers of civilization like Samarkand, Urgench, and Baghdad.  Instead, Mingburnu was prevented from fleeing into India, and was forced the face Genghis at the Battle of Indus, where his armies were again obliterated.  So if you respect his ambassadors, you should be fine visiting the Mongols—you may even get the chance to talk to Genghis himself.  Just don’t follow the path of Shah Muhammad, who is personally responsible for ending the Golden Age of Islam.

Day Six

The Transfer of Siberian Divisions to Win the Battle of Moscow, Tokyo, September 1941

In your third visit to WWII, you will journey to Tokyo to meet the man who was better at stopping Germans than the Russian winter, a spy who almost saved the Soviet Union only to be ignored by Stalin, and then saved it a second time.

Follow in the footsteps of master spy Richard Sorge, a man who served in the German Army in WWI, receiving the Iron Cross, and then converted to communism, swearing his allegiance to Mother Russia.  You will learn how Sorge managed to fool the Japanese into divulging their top secret plans not once but twice.  First you will shadow him as he discovers on June 15 the exact date of Operation Barbarossa, only for Stalin to say, “There’s this bastard who’s set up factories and brothels in Japan and even deigned to report the date of the German attack as 22 June.  Are you suggesting I should believe him too?”  Yet 7 days later, the Germans commenced the largest invasion in history, one that would almost bring the mighty Soviet Union to its knees.  Two months later, Josef Stalin and millions of Soviet soldiers were paying the price in blood.  Everything was going perfectly for Germany: the German blitzkrieg was rampaging through hundreds of miles of Russia in record time, decimating the Red Army at every turn, and had just encircled Kiev, a major industrial center.  Germany was still fighting a one-front war, but Stalin was still preparing to fight a two-front war: 31 divisions were locked up in Siberia ready to face an imminent Japanese invasion like at the Battle of Khalkhin Gol only two years earlier.  These Siberian divisions were the cream of the crop, battle-hardened veterans from their skirmishes with Japan, the best in the world at winter warfare, and led by the great Soviet general Georgy Zhukov who would one day lead the Red Army all the way to Berlin.  They would remain there, on the Mongolian border, until Stalin was assured that Japan would never invade, something he couldn’t have known until it was too late.  Now you will witness Richard Sorge save Russia a second time, discovering that Japan had cancelled their scheduled invasion of the Soviet Union on August 19.  Through his miraculous actions, he learned that Japan will not invade until Moscow has already fallen, the Kwantung Army (IJA) was triple the size of the Siberian divisions, and a civil war had started in Siberia due to the collapse of the Soviet Union.  Stalin had learned his lesson this time, and safely called all 31 elite Siberian divisions home to Moscow.  Then you watch, while driving your own iconic T-34, one of the 900 tanks from Siberia, as Georgy Zhukov and his badly needed reinforcements arrived in the nick of time to defend Moscow at all costs until winter set in.  They were able to successfully grind the Wehrmacht to a halt only 10 miles from Moscow, where, once winter began, the German war machine would cease to function.  Thanks to Richard Sorge, Stalin could have mobilized against the German invasion, avoiding the destruction of 2,000 Russian planes sitting on the ground on the first day alone.  Yet Sorge pulled through again, allowing Georgy Zhukov to return home with his army and save the day.

Day Seven

The Battle of Shanhai Pass, the Great Wall of China, May 27, 1644

Not only will you get to see one of the Seven Wonders of the World in active duty, you will also witness the day that a Chinese general opened the gate of the Great Wall in welcome arms to the Manchus, who wouldn’t leave until 1912 and only after killing 25 million Chinese.

After marveling upon the beauty that is the Great Wall of China as it marches into the sea on one end and extends for thousands of miles on the other end, you will receive the opportunity to get inside the mind of the Ming general Wu Sangui and understand how a slight delay in communications and a hasty reaction compelled him to invite Prince Dorgon and his barbaric Manchu into China, where they would kill 25 million Han in their conquest, 5% of the world’s population. First you will experience the events leading up to this cataclysmic event, from watching Li Zicheng’s successful rebellion in Beijing to seeing how, after receiving an order to surrender by Li Zicheng, Wu Sangui mistakenly took his time to decide on the correct course of action and first test the loyalty of his men.  But once he had decided to surrender his forces to the new Shun Dynasty, Lizheng had already interpreted his silence as a refusal and had murdered his family.  When Wu Sangui ran into his father’s concubine barely escaping from Beijing, telling the tale of the massacre of Wu’s family, all bets were off, and Wu vowed to defeat Li at all costs, including opening the Great Wall and inviting in the barbarians to the north, allowing them to decimate China.  Witness this amazing and horrific tale unfold with your very eyes, leading to the climactic battle between Wu Sangui, Dorgon, and the combined Qing-Ming army against Li Zicheng and the last Chinese dynasty for 300 years, all with Shanhai Pass looming in the background, the point where man’s largest monument meets the sea.

Day Eight

The Cuban Missile Crisis, October 27, 1962

Dive deep into the depths of the Caribbean to discover the tale of an unspoken hero who truly saved the world.

The Cuban Missile Crisis is the closest humanity has ever been to extinction.  Now you will get the opportunity to witness a Russian submarine commander decide the fate of the world.  Deep beneath the Caribbean, you will join the crew of B-59, a Foxtrot Soviet submarine armed with nuclear torpedoes, and experience a day in the life of a submariner, albeit a day where you will see three men fight about whether they should start WWIII.  Since B-59 had last been on the surface, tensions were rising between the two superpowers—an American U-2 reconnaissance plane had been shot down by a Soviet missile and another one had penetrated Soviet airspace only to be chased out by MiGs—and an escalated war seemed imminent.  When the US naval blockade led by the aircraft carrier USS Randolph detected B-59, they began to drop non-lethal, practice depth charges as a message for the Soviet submarine to surface.  But, as you will see, underneath hundred of meters of water, B-59 captain Valentin Savitsky never received the many messages from the US fleet, and without any communication with Moscow, assumed that WWIII had begun and ordered their 10 kiloton nuclear torpedo be readied for firing at the USS Randolph.  Here you will witness the decisive actions of flotilla commander Vasili Arkhipov that were able to prevent the onslaught of nuclear armageddon.  As emotions flared up among the crew, both Savitsky and political officer Maslennikov were prepared to fire, and although a unanimous vote was required among Savitsky, Maslennikov, and Arkhipov, the former two were willing to bypass him.  Had that torpedo been fired, the USS Randolph would have been vaporized, a cloud of deadly radiation would have descended upon land within minutes.  30 minutes later 10,000 nuclear missiles would have rained down on Earth and humanity would cease to exist.  Only by traveling back in time to the day when the world unknowingly sat awaiting its fate, when three Russian officers played God, will you learn how we all evaded extinction that fateful day.

Day Nine

The Battle of Vienna, 4:00 pm, September 11, 1683

Any fans of The Lord of the Rings will love this visit.  Travel back to when Europe was threatened by the Turkish Menace, when the massive Ottoman army was still hammering at the gates of Europe, and cavalry still ruled the day, to the September 11th that dwarfed in scale to 2001, where you witness the epic charge of the Winged Hussars, from where Tolkien derived his Rohirrim, that vanquished the last outside threat to Europe.

If you choose to journey back to Vienna in 1683, you will join our search for that little strategic move that completely changed the course of history in the most epic battle of all of history.  The massive Ottoman army under Grand Vizier Kara Mustafa Pasha, 300,000 strong and with 12,000 crack Janissaries, was at the gates of Vienna, the gateway to Europe.  Here you will watch a two-month-long siege of an entrenched Vienna defended by only 15,000, one that involved an underground battle between Ottoman and Viennese miners.  You can watch as these miners battle it out in cramped tunnels, fighting to the death to either blow up Vienna’s walls or to save them and save Europe.  One the last day of the siege, watch as Ottoman sappers prepare a huge bomb, one that would have ripped open the walls of Vienna to allow the Janissaries to pour in, only to witness a few brave Viennese defuse the bomb at the last second.  With Vienna’s capitulation avoided that day, the pan-European relief force arrived.  An army nearly 70,000 strong, composed of imperial Holy Roman troops as well as reinforcements from Saxony, Bavaria, Baden, Franconia, and Swabia (there were no French troops because Louis XIV thought Alsace was more important) on a core of 3,000 elite Winged Hussars, led by the courageous and charismatic Jan Sobieski, King of Poland, and with the zeal of Crusaders fighting in the heartland of Europe, they were prepared to tear through the now defending Ottoman army, still twice as large, and save Vienna from the Turkish Menace.  Then you will watch a time travel montage of the Winged Hussars in battle: with their gleaming full plate armor that reflected in the enemies’ faces, 16 foot-long heavy lances, and a full set of wings that made a deafening whistling sound as they charged, witness the Polish Husaria crash through and obliterate armies 10 times their sizes like it was nothing, and then repeat it 15 times.  Now knowing how badass the Winged Hussars, with whom only the Mongols are on par with, you will watch their epic charge of only 3,000 into the awaiting Ottoman army, still 150,000 strong.  Emerging from the forest along the ridge overlooking Vienna, with the sun behind their backs and their wings producing a deafening battle cry, you will witness them crash into the Ottoman ranks like they they were butter, overrunning them even faster that the Rohirrim overran the orcs, skewering soldier after soldier on their kopia (lance) faster than even Legolas could shoot down orcs, in a race to Kara Mustafa Pasha’s tent, only to turn around on a dime and charge back right through them again.  With the allied infantry closing in, the Ottomans had no chance, and they would never again see the light of day within the heart of Europe: the Turkish Menace was no more; the Sleeping Giant of Europe was sleeping no more, it had been skewered in the eye by the kopia of Jan Sobieski.  If you choose to come, you will witness the battle that makes all other pale in comparison, making a fantastical battle in The Lord of the Rings look puny in comparison.

Day Ten

The White House, Washington, D.C, USA and Yongbyon Nuclear Testing Facility, North Korea

Anyone who is old enough drink may have died if William Perry hadn’t decided to keep his mouth shut.  Journey back to this fateful day, where you will not only be able to sneak into North Korea’s secret nuclear testing facility at Yongbyon, but also witness the meeting at the White House that decided the fate of North Korea, and perhaps of the world.

In your visit to a top secret meeting in the White House between President Bill Clinton, Secretary of Defense William Perry, and many other advisors, will you witness how William Perry singlehandedly prevented the crisis of the rogue state North Korea building its own nuclear bomb from turning into a full-fledged, potentially nuclear war.  Washington was ready to start a war, seeking to bomb North Korea’s nuclear testing facility in Yongbyon with a few F-117 stealth attack aircraft and prevent it from developing its nuclear program.  The only thing stopping them was the chance that bombing the nuclear facility would cause radioactive fallout and the consequent enormous humanitarian disaster.  But William Perry had been assured by the military that the chance of radioactive fallout is negligible.  You will accompany William Perry throughout the day, from seeing him learn that the bombing would be safe, to debating whether or not to disclose this information to the president, an action that would likely lead to war against North Korea, to ultimately telling President Clinton that the bombing run was a no go, and thus preventing a potential nuclear escalation.  Being such a recent event, there is an extra perk to this visit: Bill Clinton will accompany you on your trip.  As you tail William Perry throughout the day, Bill Clinton will be explaining all the possible scenarios going through Perry’s head, as well as remarking his thoughts at the time to how close America came to launching a war against a potentially nuclear power with strong nuclear allies.

Day Eleven

Tuesday, November 8, 2016

Today we still face our own decisions that may one day decide the fate of the world.  Will the next one be on Election Day?  Imagine Donald Trump in the White House.  Your vote might be that tiny strategic decision that completely changes the course of the future.

This year’s upcoming election might your chance to take part in one of these strategic decisions that shaped the course of history.  Make sure you choose wisely.  If Donald Trump is elected, will he act like Genghis Khan and when he learns about the trade deficit (aren’t we capitalist not mercantilist anyways?) or about an American journalist being beheaded by ISIS, launches an unilateral war to teach the world a lesson?  Will he act like Napoleon and claim to be an amazing businessman but then arrogantly dismiss other economic proposals that cost us our economy?  He definitely won’t be like Wu Sangui and open the Mexican border and invite the “criminals, drug dealers, and rapists” in, he’ll be like Qin Shi Huangdi and build his own wall.  But most importantly, will he act like William Perry or Vasili Arkhipov and prevent nuclear war at all costs, or will he be prepared to use it like a certain unnamed US translator who mistranslated the Japanese response to the Potsdam Declaration, “mokusatsu” as “ignore” instead of “no comment” as caused the first two and only nuclear bombings.
