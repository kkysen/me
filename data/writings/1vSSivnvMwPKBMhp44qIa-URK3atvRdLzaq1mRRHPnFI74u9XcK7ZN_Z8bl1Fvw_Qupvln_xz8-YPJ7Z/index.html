Dear Smith (Hume, Klibansky, and Mossner 51)

I have delayed responding to your last letter until I could make something of the recent events in France. Forgive me but I’ll go on something of a rant here; it is a lot to take in and puts quite a lot of meaning to the French people.

Something crucial, I believe, that should be addressed is the work of Abbe Emmanuel Joseph Sieyes. Certainly, he began something crucial with his work of “Qu'est-ce que le tiers-état?  (Sieyes, “What is the Third Estate?”). In this essay (which I highly recommend you read—it gives excellent context for what happens later on—I’m sure you’re well-informed at this point), Sieyes outlines what he believes the Third Estate, the people below nobility and the self-righteous, so-called “religious leaders” who are in the second and first estates, respectively, should be within the French society. For speculation, I believe this essay holds some logical sense. First and foremost, we should, of course, disregard the authority of the clergy. They hold this aforementioned authority only because of divinity and their association with God. Society today is above these frivolous miracles that are created only within the context of our primitive fear of instability (Hume, “The Natural History of Religion”. Section V); their authority is no longer necessary within a governed state at all because people have created God, not the other way around (Hume, “An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding”). While I see that Sieyes finds great injustice in the state, I do not agree with his standpoints. The ideas are too radical, much like the Whigs in England (Coniff, “Hume On Political Parties: The Case for Hume as a Whig”).  They are ignorant of the importance of the monarchy (Fiefer, “David Hume (1711-1776). Introduction). Sieyes disregards the authority of the upper class and monarchy. The letter seems to empower the common people, but in a anarchical and ideal manner that can only result in chaos because it does not give power to the people as individuals before the state, but gives power to everyone without a proper state to govern them. Moreover, it calls for revolutionary ideas and alterations to the state, but, in reality, this should be dissolved with political moderation (Gallegos, “Hume on Revolution). This is the reason I agree with King Louis’ Cahiers de doleances and the Estates-general that began, seemingly, as a call for compromise between the two political groups. Both, however, failed in their motives. The king failed to exercise and assert his power and the people failed to assert a constitutional monarchy (Hume, “Political Writings” Essay IV). Surely, that is not the fault of the people; the king was too incompetent to realize and control the problems of the people. This resulted, inevitably, in the beginning of a revolution. The Tennis Court Oaths (the members of the third estate gathered to swear allegiance to each other and against the king), upon the king’s meek attempt to prevent the angered third estate from meeting, escalated and forced the revolution forward, affirming its dreadful occurrence. Counterintuitively, the king managed to raise the problems more with his attempts to solve them, resulting in the attack on Bastille, by July 14, 1789. I do not believe, that these people should be given the right to revolt unless in a state of tyranny, otherwise war is a fight between political parties and not where justice is meant to prevail (Gallegos, “Hume of Revolution”). The third estate represents, not a group of intellectually organized individuals, but a group of people armed with passions that cause increased instability in this war. Women empowered themselves, stepped outside of what society has made them. While that is good, I question the genuinity in their pursuit, whether the March was worth it in itself (Garrett, “Hume as a Man of Reason Women’s Philosopher”) . While the idea of a constitution was well-thought out, the execution of it so far has implied disorganization. The declaration of the Rights of Man has perpetuated the problem: allowing individuals to fight as if they act upon their own government. This gives way to more power abuse, the very thing they want to stop.

So far, something good that has occurred is the change in religious policy. Giving church land over to the government and making the religious leaders obey the law is an advancement; it shifts us away from treating certain individuals incorrectly with regard to superiority (Kippis, “The New Annual Register” Parliamentary Debates). 

Taking a step back now, I realize, there is a relation between our previous discussion regarding the economy. I agree, the people of France are very unsatisfied with the economy (it adds large amounts of fire to this rebellion). In this respect, I believe the rebellion will allow for more money to be spent outside of the country because of recent political reforms. It is crucial to deconstruct mercantilism, primarily because it fails to recognize that increases in money within the country will always exit the country through means such as trade, it is insensible to store up money inside the country. It is healthy, and necessary, to cycle money up and down the economy.

“If I add fluid to one chamber, then, under the weight of gravity, this will disperse to the others until the level is the same in all chambers. A similar phenomenon will occur if we lose money in our home country by purchasing imports from foreign countries. As the quantity of money decreases in our home country, this will drive down the prices of labor and domestic products. Our products, then, will be cheaper than foreign products, and we will gain money through exports. On the fluid analogy, by removing fluid from one chamber, more fluid is drawn in from surrounding chambers.” (Hume, “Political Discourses”. Balance of Trade)

I hope my letter isn’t too muddled, for there is a lot to say. I look forward to your response and to further discuss these matters with you.

Dear sir

Your most oblig’d humble Servant

David Hume (Hume and Greig 32-35)

University of Glasgow. 19 June. 1791

Dear Hume

It is so good to hear from you again.  I just recently returned from touring Europe as a tutor of Henry Scott, the Duke of Buccleuch, a trip during which I even traveled to France.  I left France before all these tumultuous events you speak of ever occurred, but to imagine I had just been there and it had been a perfectly normal country?  I wrote this letter in a hurry—I only received it a few days ago—so forgive me if my thoughts are a bit long-winded and all out of order; I didn’t have much time to articulate and organize my thoughts clearly.  But after I heard about the new Le Chapelier Law (I’ll get to that a bit later), and having not heard from you in a while, I had to write back as soon as possible.

My first reaction to your letter is that in Sieyes’ “What is the Third Estate?”, I believe he is completely justified in condemning the excessive privileges of the First and Second Estate.  The government has no right to give a group of people such a privilege that allows them to oppress the Third Estate.  The government should leave the people alone, and should not grant privileges that will disrupt the laissez-faire free market.  The invisible hand will be sufficient to guide all the people if they act in their own self-interest, and thus there is no need for government meddling (Smith, “An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations”).  With such excessive privileges granted hereditarily and not out of merit, the nobility will oppress the Third Estate, and thus restrain the free competition that creates a stronger economy.  If the entire Third Estate is freed according to Sieyes’ vision, then it will free more than 95% of the French people to contribute to the economy.  The disastrous effects of these privileges is readily evident in France’s current economic state under Louis XVI and terrible, lying Finance Ministers like Necker.  France’s economy is crumbling, and unless the full might of the entire population is mobilized to revitalize the economy, with the government sitting back and allowing the French people themselves to restore to France a booming economy, the situation will only worsen (although a major part of France’s current economic recession is due to them funding the American Revolution, for it is disadvantageous to have colonies to begin with, and funding a colony that is not even your own is complete and utter stupidity) (Smith, “An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations”).

I definitely do agree with your points about the clergy.  The capital wasted in religious endeavors is great, and they definitely should not receive privileges for being part of an institution that is tantamount to exalted superstition (Coase 538), especially the “stupid and lying missionaries” (Smith, “An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations” V.1.87).  But the rest of Sieyes’ points are equally valid.  What makes them so radical?  It would definitely be beneficial to France to abolish the First and Second Estates and destroy all feudal remnants that retard the power of the free market.  As I explained above, by throwing off the yoke of the oppressive nobility, the Third Estate would be able to turn around France’s precipitously declining economy by completely opening the free market to France’s massive population; once there is free competition, everyone working in their self-interest will be sufficient to reverse the economic collapse now seen in France (Smith, “An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations”).

Furthermore, why are his ideas against such a strong monarchy so detrimental?  The government serves only three main purposes: to protect against violence and invasion, to maintain justice, and to erect and maintain public institutions and works (Smith, “An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations” V.1.41, 44, 69-70).  Their main role is in abstaining from interfering in the economy, for by letting it decide its own course, it will undoubtedly seek out the best path.  Sieyes is empowering the common people—that’s true—but this will not promote anarchy and chaos; it will only serve to allow all the people to freely compete in the market, thereby greatly improving the economy (Smith, “An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations”).  You also advocated for many of these same principles the French are now fighting for during the American Revolution, and you are also a fan of our own Glorious Revolution (Gallegos, “Hume on Revolution”), so I do not understand your opposition to Sieyes’ ideas, however rebellious they are.

As for your claim that it is rebellious, that is correct only in limitation.  The Tennis Court Oath proved this.  The Third Estate, after they were prevented from properly assembling, decided to assemble elsewhere and take an oath that they will not stop assembling until the king ratifies the constitution that they will write.  All they are trying to do is establish a constitutional monarchy, one just like we have here in Great Britain.  Currently, Great Britain is the foremost economic power in Europe, and the correlation to a constitutional monarchy cannot be ignored.  But France, with its massive population of 28 million people, has the potential to take this position from England, if it is able to establish a truly free market guided only by the invisible hand and never by the government, and a necessary step in achieving this is the liberation of the entire population of France, by which everyone will be able to engage in free competition; no longer will the free market be so dominated by the nobility and even the bourgeoisie (Smith, “An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations”).

The Storming of the Bastille and the Women’s March on Versailles were, I admit, unfortunate events in achieving this realization of a truly free French market. By moving the beginnings of the revolution towards violence, they will waste significant economic resources in fighting a war, while achieving their aims could perhaps have been carried out with less bloodshed.  As for the Women’s March on Versailles, the entire cause of their march was inherently flawed.  They marched on the palace demanding lower prices of bread, calling for a fixed price of grain and bread.  But the supply of bread was running low, and fixing its price only worsened the situation.  The price rises because there is limited supply, and in order to maintain their business, the farmers must raise the price.  Having a higher price will then attract others to take up the same profession, who see a chance for profit in selling the grain at such a high price.  This will consequently increase the supply, thus resulting in a lowered price.  But by fixing the price, farmers will only lose their business, and consequently produce less grain, which will only raise grain prices further.  The natural price of grain will remain constant, but the market price of grain will fluctuate in response to the supply and demand of grain.  When the effectual demand increases or the actual quantity of a commodity brought to the market is decreased, competition among the buyers will increase the market price, and the opposite occurs when the demand decreases or supply increases.  Therefore, the Women’s March on Versailles only served to further compound their plight.  The lost money that the farmers receive in payment for the grain may be subsidized by the government, but that just reallocates the debt to the national government (Smith, “An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations”).

The nationalization of church property and the Civil Constitution of the Clergy were certainly steps in the right direction, as you thought, but there was a major, hidden economic flaw that resulted: the Assignats.  The National Assembly was faced with an enormous national debt that only continued to grow, but in promising reform, they couldn’t further raise taxes.  Consequently, with previously Church-owned lands now in government hands, they believed they could exploit this expropriated land.

Normally, this would have crossed a line in maintaining the right to property.  This does not only apply to individuals, but must apply also to institutional property, such as that of the Church, and therefore the government cannot simply expropriate property without venturing into despotism.  Property rights are a major foundation of jurisprudence; even a starving man who steals bread must be held accountable and be brought to justice.  While the punishment might be mitigated given the extenuating circumstances (Smith, “The Theory of Moral Sentiments”), it is still a crime.  Likewise, it should be a crime for the National Assembly to forcibly seize all the church property.  However, in times of emergency, the state is sui generis, and it is allowed to violate the otherwise inviolable property rights to save the nation.  This would apply to this case of nationalizing Church property if the resultant Assignats rescue the nation from its crumbling economy, but the issuance of Assignats only exacerbated France’s economic situation (Sagar, “Adam Smith on Property rights and His Good Sense In the Real World”; Smith, “An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations”).

This was especially true once the National Assembly decided to make the Assignats full legal tender and print 1200 million of them at denominations of 200, 300, and 1,000 livres (Trask, “Inflation and the French Revolution: The Story of a Monetary Catastrophe”).  There must be a limit on the size of these banknotes and the enormous quantity printed, and the government must be able immediately exchange the Assignats for real money or something of equivalent, unchanging value (Rockoff, “Adam Smith on Money, Banking, and the Price Level”).  They cannot do this, because the Assignats sold were not backed up by anything of value except the land that was expropriated from the Church.  But the value of this land has a limit, and the National Assembly decided to just keep printing more Assignats without acquiring further land.  Therefore, this will cause hyperinflation, and the Assignats will soon become practically worthless, and this will be yet another economic disaster for France (Trask, “Inflation and the French Revolution: The Story of a Monetary Catastrophe”; Smith, “An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations”).

But enough with all the negative aspects of revolutionary France.  Have you heard about the new Le Chapelier Law?  It was ratified only a few days ago.  It has abolished all guilds outlawed the right to strike (Le Chapelier)!  Finally they are moving on the path to an improved economy, advocating for free enterprise, instead of a market controlled by the despicable guilds that oppose free trade, including acting against people of the same profession who try to trade freely, and hinder the economy by not allowing for a free market.  But now there can be a free market in France, and that is truly revolutionary for a nation that in the past was an ardent advocator for that damned mercantilism.

In reply to your linkage between the revolution and France’s economy, you already definitely agree with you.  I’ve written many letters espousing this exact economic thinking.  But one major problem with the revolution in respect towards the economy is that, fearing the fall of monarchy in France spreading throughout Europe, the revolution has made France the enemy of all the monarchs of Europe, and if war were to break out between them, it would negate any economic advantages incurred due to the newly created free market in France.  The capital that will need to be spent defending France will be necessary but costly, but if France becomes the aggressor, all that goes out the window, and France’s economy will suffer under the strains of war as trade with the other Great Powers of Europe is disrupted.  So let’s hope that the revolution will be able to resolve itself without dragging France into a war against Europe that will devastate all their economies.

I ever am, dear Sir,

Most affectionately yours,

Adam Smith (Smith, “The Death of David Hume”).

Lisle Street Leicester Fields London. 30 January. 1791

This, I believe, is the beginning of more and unnecessary violence. The King had already agreed to the Constitution of 1791. This was an excellent and crucial idea; it retained the necessary components of a strong, centralized monarchy with justified representation for the people. The division of power seemed parallel with the British government, but, yet, the people remained unsatisfied. Insofar as they have a chaotic system, they are risk of instability in their own nation, their rights will hold no utility outside. With the King and no system of monarchy to continue, the government is open for abuse; those with power who want to take it could seize control of the military and set another cycle of  tyranny. The function of the government, indeed, is the matter of its utility for the people. Therefore, you must agree with me when I say that a strong government must be maintained, with avoidance of conflict with other countries. While the government is still in formation, it seems to be in the nation’s best interest not to cause tensions with outside nations, yet the French people have ignored the Declaration of Pillnitz and the Brunswick Manifesto, and harmed the King and Queen, the latter who is the sister of the Austrian King, Leopold the Second who combined powers with the Prussian king. Insofar as you have mentioned that I support rebellion, I do so only when the abuses are extreme and the people suffer immensely and only in cases where the rebellion is strategic (Gallegos, “Hume of Revolution”). While this rebellion began strategic, its plans have fallen into disarray and this makes me fear the future of the French government. While I believe in utilizing war when necessary, a nation just born does not have the strength, even if they win the wars, to maintain itself with merely quick and unstable positions of power; they should have sought compromises with the countries against them, instead of declaring war.

        In reply to your last letter, I agree with your points on France’s potential to rise above England in terms of economic power, yet I do not believe they are able to. Perhaps, though (and I hope), that I will be proven wrong, in which case we shall exchange relieved messages, and the government of France will be able to rise from its violent beginnings of a new nation (and a pure republic at that) and stabilize itself. In regard to your statement concerning property rights, I choose to disagree; the giving of church property in the beginning was unauthorized due to the fact that the only justification involved religion. While taking it away may be seen as unjust, the justice is dependent on the time period and necessities of the government of other groups to that property. Seeing as the government needed land and re-establishments, that is sufficient justification for the seizing of church land which they can later buy back, or individuals can (Waldron, “Property and Ownership” Section 1).

University of Glasgow. 26 February. 1793

Dear Hume

It has been good to hear from you again.  I sympathize with your viewpoint in lamenting the path the French people have taken in executing Louis XVI, abolishing the monarchy, and declaring a republic, but there a few points that I believe you have missed.

Firstly, you asked me what shall occur without a strong leader, a centralized group to govern the masses, and I answer that when there is an absolute monarch controlling every aspect of a country, he is taking it into his own hands to distribute resources.  He does not have to produce anything of value in surplus, and then through truck, barter, and exchange, acquire something else that is of more value to himself.  He is usurping the power of the invisible hand, of the innate human predisposition to truck, barter, and exchange.  When the economy is left to be run by the invisible hand, through the division of labor capitalizing on a fundamental aspect of human nature, it is at its best, not at the hands of a despotic ruler.  It is the collective free market of the people at the base of society that makes capitalism run smoothly, and I know I’ve long since convinced you of the advantages of capitalism over mercantilism or any other economic system (Rollert, “Does the Top Really Support the Bottom?—Adam Smith and the Problem of the Commercial Pyramid”; Smith, “An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations”).  You are completely correct in stressing the need for a stable government, for without stability, the government cannot fulfill its purpose, and it has only three of them.  But a stable one need not be a strong one, as long as it fulfill these three purposes: to protect the nation, to maintain justice, and maintain public institutions and works (Smith, “An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations” V.1.41, 44, 69-70).

Secondly, you dared to say that the government comes before the economy.  Even if you really believe you’re right, I can’t believe you had the nerve to even suggest such a preposterous idea to me.  Of course the economy is more important than the government.  Economy does not require a government, it will just inherently arise when there are at least two people in the world, and they each have something the other wants, and consequently decide to trade.  Government, as long as it is limited and doesn’t interfere with the economy, will definitely help the economy by fulfilling the three purposes I listed above (Smith, “An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations” V.1.41, 44, 69-70), but it certainly cannot claim to be more important than economy.

Thirdly, you seem to despise the wars against Austria and Prussia.  I myself am not a huge fan either, and I regret the reasons causing the French to enter into such a war, but although I have often repeated how it was a monumental mistake for England to go to war against America, and how they shouldn’t have even colonized it in the first place, this war in France is fundamentally different.  Against America, England was fighting across an entire ocean.  There was never any threat to England itself, and thus, since it had no need to defend itself, it shouldn’t have gone to war.  Instead, they only massively increased their war debt transporting troops across the Atlantic to fight a war it didn’t even matter to them at home if they won (Smith, “An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations”).  They had a professional army, and thus paid their soldiers well.  But in France, the Revolutionary Army is paid poorly, and what it is paid, it has been so inflated by the Assignats to become practically worthless and often paid many months in arrears as well (Forrest 40, 50, 175), and most importantly, they are fighting to defend their nation against the Austrian and Prussian invaders.  Have you not read La Patrie en Danger?  I agree that France didn’t necessarily have to get dragged into this war in the first place, but it is definitely a just war, especially considering that there is so much rampant French nationalism and patriotism that the citizens, in particular the sans-culottes, who practically go to war, sacrificing their life for their nation, for free (I mean, there are hordes of entire volunteer divisions), which means that the war is much less of a burden on the economy than the American Revolutionary War and it is for a vastly more important cause.

Fourthly, you also even had the nerve to say that you think it is wise for France to be funding America.  Have I not told you so many times in the past how it was a disaster for England?  And France didn’t even own the colonies; they received nothing in return.  You’re suggesting that they might become a French ally, but America is across the ocean.  How can they help the plight of the French from across the Atlantic when they are so young and have such a puny army and navy.  When England tried to do the same thing, they failed, even though they arguably have the strongest military, and most definitely the best navy, in the world.  This is money ill spent, and it would be wiser to invest it in France’s own economy, for you need to spend money to make money, and America is definitely not making money for France, while their own people surely could improve their economy.

Fifthly, you have also claimed that when a new nation is created, it doesn’t have the strength to fight wars immediately.  I choose to disagree.  Most new nations are born of war, of a revolutionary war, and thus are in a prime position to wage war afterwards.  Then you said that even if they can win wars, they cannot maintain themselves and establish stable positions of power.  But have you not studied the great empires of shepherds.  Have you not heard of the Tartar and Arab shepherd militias (Smith, “An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations” V.1.25)?  After centuries of civil war, they both created a new state, and went on to create the two largest empires known to man, that of the Arabs united by Mohammed (Smith, “An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations” V.1.4) being nearly tied with the current Spanish Empire, and that of the Tartars united by Genghis Khan (Smith, “An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations” V.1.4) being larger than even the mighty British Empire.  Although the Tartars couldn’t last two centuries (which is still a very long time), the Arabs created the Islamic Caliphates, whose incarnation still recently existed in the empires of the Ottomans, Safavids, and Mughals from the Atlantic to the far reaches of India.  The Safavids have declined, but the other two still remain, and have had stable governments for many centuries.  The Ottomans have lasted for five centuries, and are not showing signs of stopping any time soon.  So when a nation born of war has lasted that long and has been that prosperous, how can you claim such a thing?

Lastly, you have responded to my thoughts on the nationalization of Church property with such fury that I believe you misunderstood me.  I said that under normal circumstances, such a seizure violates the inviolability of property, and should thus be condemned, but this is a rare exception, for it is a matter of a state emergency.  However, despite being a state emergency, the National Assembly used it to print Assignats, a disaster that only worsened the economy, the very thing they were trying to improve through their emergency seizure of property.  Therefore, it was a huge mistake to invoke emergency powers to violate the otherwise inviolability of property, the basis of trade, and thus the foundation of capitalism (Smith, “An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations”).

I ever am, dear Sir,

Most affectionately yours,

Adam Smith (Smith, “The Death of David Hume”)

Dear Sir

In light of recent events, I am sure that there are certain opinions you shall change from your last letter. My fear of violence, of meaningless bloodshed has, indeed, come true.

In response to your belief that the economy is better without a centralized monarchy, I believe I can answer both this and your point regarding the economy coming before the government with the Reign of Terror. Indeed, the Committee of Public Safety and the Jacobins had given the people more economic freedom, but the extension of time and war has had them place France into a battle between political parties. The government is essential to the functionality of the economy, if the Terror continues to assert itself over the French people, it will be impossible to raise the economy. That is, essentially, why I believe the government (an establishment of a good one, at least) must come before the economy, especially in the face of a recent revolution. Now, we can see the result: the country has devolved into bloody executions on guillotines that do not ring with justice. Insofar as any government has to contain a struggle between liberty and power, liberty justifies rebellion so long as it is achieved and, currently, no liberty has been established, economic or social (“David Hume on the origin of government in warfare, and the “perpetual struggle” between Liberty and Power”). You can tell me that economical values will rise if there remains only two individuals in the world, but I believe that is a flaw in your argument for two people are an insensible representation of the masses. Since we do not live in such a world (if we did, it would appear that we are the only two correspondents that speculate with each other), it is crucial to realize that while some conception of the economy can exist without a government, economic freedom cannot exist without the government, otherwise it would just be people trading in a state of anarchy. This is why I believe in a mixed constitution, for it is the best representation of both the people and their psychological need to follow a powerful individual, the biggest justification for why a monarchy is essential for the people (Fieser, “David Hume (1711-1776).

You mention how a country is most powerful after a war and I can agree with that, but since the French government has not been established and, now, the country is left to fight another bloody battle within themselves, the people are not powerful and the recent Levee en masse is another perpetuation of this violence. When, I wonder will this violence stop? The Constitution of 1793 holds some disturbing implications and, does not, help the case of the French. This constitution gives them the right to revolt and to assert oneself over the sovereignty. A right to revolt is very vague, rebellion should not be this easily justified. To assert oneself over sovereignty is to force power to win over liberty, what shall happen when each individual wants the highest position? The answer: The current state of France. Whence does a country emerge from its war-like origins? La Constitution Francaise. Declaration dea droits de l’homme et du Citoyen”).  The Constitution of 1793 was significantly more radical, having been written by the radical Montagnards (Collot d’Herbois et al., “Acte constitutionnel precede de la declaration des droits de l'homme et du citoyen : presente au peuple francais, par la Convention nationale, le 24 juin 1793, l'an deuxieme de la republique”), rather than the Constitution of 1791, which still proposed a constitutional monarchy rather than a republic  (L’Assemblee Nationale de France, “Decret de L’Assemblee Nationale. du trois Septembre 1791. La Constitution Francaise. Declaration dea droits de l’homme et du Citoyen”).  Accordingly, it decreed that there be a uniform tax paid by all citizens, regardless of everything (Collot d’Herbois et al., “Acte constitutionnel precede de la declaration des droits de l'homme et du citoyen : presente au peuple francais, par la Convention nationale, le 24 juin 1793, l'an deuxieme de la republique” Article 101).  But then the somewhat recent Constitution of 1795 took a step backwards.  It attempted to find a middle ground between the radical Jacobins and traditional royalists, and thus was liberally conservative (Convention Nationale de France, “Constitution de la République Française du 5 Fructidor l'an III”).  Therefore, while it never recreated the distinctions between active and passive citizens  (Convention Nationale de France, “Constitution de la République Française du 5 Fructidor l'an III”), it alluded to a similar franchise based on the payment of taxes, which stems from wealth, in saying that the contributions, meaning taxes, of all kinds are distributed among all taxpayers in proportions to their means, meaning in proportion to their wealth  (Convention Nationale de France, “Constitution de la République Française du 5 Fructidor l'an III” Article 306), which insinuates a franchise based on wealth.

In my book The Wealth of Nations, I have outlined four basic maxims for taxation, which can be summarized as equity, transparency, convenience, and efficiency.  If all four of these tax maxims are abided by, in addition to levying the least amount of taxes to support a limited government (Walton, “Adam Smith on Taxes”) that serves its three main purposes, to defend the state, to maintain justice, and maintain public institutions and works (Smith, “An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations” V.1.41, 44, 69-70), as well as maintaining the free market, then the nation will maximize its potential wealth.  However, the Constitution of 1795 is not specific in any of these areas except taxing the people equitably (Convention Nationale de France, “Constitution de la République Française du 5 Fructidor l'an III”), so that is the only maxim I will focus on.  When the Constitution stipulates in Article 306 that the contributions of any kind are distributed among all taxpayers in proportion to their means, it is basically saying that all taxes are to be paid in proportion to an individual’s ability to pay, meaning equitably.  

This is similar to how when a carriage passes over a highway or a bridge, or how when a lighter sails upon a navigable canal, it must pay a toll, which is basically a tax, in proportion to their weight or tonnage.  Therefore, they are paying for the maintenance of these public works exactly in proportion to the wear and tear which they occasion of them, and this is the most equitable way to fund the government so that it can maintain such works.  This tax is eventually transferred to the consumer, for an increased toll results in an increased price of the goods, and thus consumers equitably pay the taxes on the goods that they buy.  However, the price of transportation is far more reduced by the public works than it is by the toll that is paid to use them, and thus it is still advantageous to both the transporter and buyer, because they gain from the works more than they lose from the toll they pay for it.  Thus this is the most equitable method of taxing, to pay in proportion to the wear and tear which they occasion of the public works (Smith, “An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations” V.1.74).

Such equitable taxing can also be applied to the annual property and income tax that is stipulated in Article 303 of the Constitution.  It is the job of the government to defend the nation, maintain justice, and erect and maintain public institutions and works, but the government must be somehow funded, and this is done through taxes.  But since the government serves the rich more than the poor, for the government protects the greater wealth and income of the rich from crime and invasion and grants them greater privileges in using the public institutions and works, it is most equitable for the rich to contribute a greater amount to the funding of the government.  The rich are paying more taxes because they are paying taxes in proportion to the security of their wealth that they occasion of the government.
